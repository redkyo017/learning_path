## Section 1: Core Psychology Terms

**psychology** *(noun)* — /sy-KOL-uh-jee/
Definition: The scientific study of the mind, mental processes, and human behaviour.
Example: "Students who major in psychology gain a thorough understanding of how mental processes influence everyday decisions."
Collocations: study psychology | applied psychology | clinical psychology

**psychological** *(adjective)* — /sy-kuh-LOJ-ih-kul/
Definition: Relating to the mind or mental processes rather than the body.
Example: "The researchers noted a significant psychological impact on participants who experienced prolonged social isolation."
Collocations: psychological effects | psychological wellbeing | psychological disorder

**psychologist** *(noun)* — /sy-KOL-uh-jist/
Definition: A trained professional who studies or treats human behaviour and mental processes.
Example: "The clinical psychologist designed a personalised treatment plan for the patient suffering from severe anxiety."
Collocations: clinical psychologist | research psychologist | consult a psychologist

**cognition** *(noun)* — /kog-NISH-un/
Definition: All mental processes involved in acquiring knowledge, including thinking, remembering, and problem-solving.
Example: "Ageing can affect cognition, particularly the speed at which the brain processes new information."
Collocations: higher cognition | impaired cognition | social cognition

**cognitive** *(adjective)* — /KOG-nih-tiv/
Definition: Relating to mental processes such as attention, memory, reasoning, and language.
Example: "Cognitive abilities such as working memory and attention are central to academic performance."
Collocations: cognitive development | cognitive ability | cognitive load

**perception** *(noun)* — /pur-SEP-shun/
Definition: The process of organising and interpreting sensory information to make sense of the environment.
Example: "Our perception of colour can be influenced by surrounding context and prior experience."
Collocations: visual perception | depth perception | perception of risk

**perceptual** *(adjective)* — /pur-SEP-choo-ul/
Definition: Relating to the way in which something is perceived or interpreted through the senses.
Example: "Perceptual errors in aviation can have fatal consequences if pilots misread altitude information."
Collocations: perceptual process | perceptual distortion | perceptual learning

**sensation** *(noun)* — /sen-SAY-shun/
Definition: The raw, uninterpreted experience received through sensory receptors before the brain processes it.
Example: "The sensation of pain serves as a biological warning system that alerts the body to potential harm."
Collocations: physical sensation | pleasant sensation | sensation of touch

**sensory** *(adjective)* — /SEN-sur-ee/
Definition: Relating to the five senses or to the sensory organs that detect stimuli from the environment.
Example: "Children with autism often have heightened sensory sensitivity, making crowded environments overwhelming."
Collocations: sensory input | sensory processing | sensory overload

**attention** *(noun)* — /uh-TEN-shun/
Definition: The cognitive ability to selectively focus on one aspect of the environment while ignoring others.
Example: "Divided attention across multiple devices has been shown to reduce the depth of information processing."
Collocations: pay attention | sustained attention | divided attention

**selective attention** *(noun phrase)* — /suh-LEK-tiv uh-TEN-shun/
Definition: The capacity to focus on a specific stimulus while filtering out other competing stimuli.
Example: "The cocktail party effect demonstrates selective attention, as listeners can pick out their own name in a noisy room."
Collocations: exercise selective attention | selective attention task | failure of selective attention

**consciousness** *(noun)* — /KON-shus-nes/
Definition: The state of being aware of and able to think about one's own existence, thoughts, and surroundings.
Example: "Philosophers and neuroscientists continue to debate whether consciousness can be fully explained by brain activity."
Collocations: altered consciousness | loss of consciousness | stream of consciousness

**unconscious** *(adjective/noun)* — /un-KON-shus/
Definition: Relating to mental processes that occur without a person's awareness or deliberate control.
Example: "Many of our daily habits are driven by unconscious processes rather than deliberate thought."
Collocations: unconscious bias | unconscious motive | the unconscious mind

**subconscious** *(adjective/noun)* — /sub-KON-shus/
Definition: Relating to thoughts, feelings, or desires that exist just below the level of conscious awareness.
Example: "Advertisers often target subconscious associations between their products and positive emotional experiences."
Collocations: subconscious mind | subconscious desire | subconscious influence

**introspection** *(noun)* — /in-truh-SPEK-shun/
Definition: The examination of one's own thoughts, feelings, and mental states.
Example: "Introspection is a central method in early psychology, though it was criticised for lacking scientific objectivity."
Collocations: engage in introspection | accurate introspection | limits of introspection

## Section 2: Memory

**memory** *(noun)* — /MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: The mental faculty that enables information to be encoded, stored, and retrieved at a later time.
Example: "Research suggests that sleep plays a vital role in consolidating memory after a period of intense learning."
Collocations: short-term memory | long-term memory | memory loss

**encoding** *(noun)* — /en-KOH-ding/
Definition: The first stage of memory in which incoming information is converted into a format that the brain can process.
Example: "Deep encoding, which involves connecting new information to existing knowledge, leads to stronger memory traces."
Collocations: memory encoding | semantic encoding | visual encoding

**storage** *(noun)* — /STOR-ij/
Definition: The retention of encoded information in the brain over a period of time.
Example: "Long-term storage of memories is believed to depend on structural changes at the synapse level."
Collocations: memory storage | long-term storage | information storage

**retrieval** *(noun)* — /rih-TREE-vul/
Definition: The process of accessing and bringing stored information back into conscious awareness.
Example: "Retrieval cues, such as familiar smells or sounds, can trigger vivid memories of past events."
Collocations: memory retrieval | retrieval cue | retrieval failure

**short-term memory** *(noun phrase)* — /SHORT-turn MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: A limited-capacity memory system that holds a small amount of information for a brief period, typically 15–30 seconds.
Example: "Short-term memory can typically hold seven items, plus or minus two, according to Miller's classic research."
Collocations: short-term memory capacity | short-term memory test | store in short-term memory

**long-term memory** *(noun phrase)* — /LONG-turn MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: A relatively permanent memory system that can hold vast amounts of information for extended periods.
Example: "Skills such as riding a bicycle are stored in long-term memory and can be retained for decades without practice."
Collocations: long-term memory formation | long-term memory retrieval | consolidate into long-term memory

**working memory** *(noun phrase)* — /WUR-king MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: A cognitive system that temporarily holds and manipulates information for use in complex tasks.
Example: "Children with a larger working memory capacity tend to perform better on mathematical reasoning tasks."
Collocations: working memory capacity | working memory load | strain working memory

**forgetting** *(noun)* — /fur-GET-ing/
Definition: The loss or inability to retrieve previously stored information from memory.
Example: "The forgetting curve, proposed by Ebbinghaus, shows that most forgetting occurs rapidly after initial learning."
Collocations: rate of forgetting | forgetting curve | motivated forgetting

**interference** *(noun)* — /in-tur-FEER-unts/
Definition: A memory phenomenon in which one piece of information disrupts the recall of another.
Example: "Learning two similar languages simultaneously can cause interference, making it harder to distinguish between them."
Collocations: proactive interference | retroactive interference | interference effect

**consolidation** *(noun)* — /kun-sol-ih-DAY-shun/
Definition: The process by which newly acquired memories become stable and long-lasting in the brain.
Example: "Memory consolidation is thought to occur primarily during slow-wave sleep when the hippocampus replays daily experiences."
Collocations: memory consolidation | consolidation process | sleep-dependent consolidation

**recall** *(noun/verb)* — /REE-kawl/
Definition: The ability to retrieve information from memory without being given a direct prompt or cue.
Example: "Free recall tasks, in which participants list as many items as possible, are commonly used to test memory capacity."
Collocations: free recall | recall accuracy | total recall

**recognition** *(noun)* — /rek-ug-NISH-un/
Definition: The ability to identify previously encountered information when it is presented again.
Example: "Recognition is generally easier than recall because the stimulus itself serves as its own retrieval cue."
Collocations: face recognition | pattern recognition | recognition test

**implicit memory** *(noun phrase)* — /IM-plih-sit MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: Memory for skills and knowledge that is accessed automatically without conscious effort.
Example: "Implicit memory allows experienced drivers to navigate familiar routes while simultaneously holding a conversation."
Collocations: implicit memory test | implicit memory system | rely on implicit memory

**explicit memory** *(noun phrase)* — /eks-PLIS-it MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: Conscious, intentional recollection of facts or events that can be directly recalled.
Example: "Explicit memory is tested when students recall specific dates and names in a history examination."
Collocations: explicit memory recall | explicit memory task | decline in explicit memory

**procedural memory** *(noun phrase)* — /pruh-SEE-jur-ul MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: A type of long-term memory that stores how to perform motor skills and routine procedures.
Example: "Procedural memory for typing develops with practice and eventually operates without conscious attention."
Collocations: procedural memory system | procedural memory task | procedural memory intact

**semantic memory** *(noun phrase)* — /suh-MAN-tik MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: Long-term memory for general knowledge and facts about the world, independent of personal experience.
Example: "Knowing that Paris is the capital of France is an example of semantic memory that most people share."
Collocations: semantic memory network | semantic memory retrieval | semantic memory deficit

**episodic memory** *(noun phrase)* — /ep-ih-SOD-ik MEM-uh-ree/
Definition: Long-term memory for personally experienced events, including their time, place, and emotional context.
Example: "The ability to vividly recall one's first day at school is a clear example of episodic memory in action."
Collocations: episodic memory recall | autobiographical episodic memory | episodic memory impairment

**priming** *(noun)* — /PRY-ming/
Definition: The phenomenon whereby exposure to one stimulus influences the response to a later stimulus without conscious awareness.
Example: "Priming studies show that reading the word 'doctor' makes people respond faster to the word 'nurse'."
Collocations: semantic priming | perceptual priming | priming effect

**amnesia** *(noun)* — /am-NEE-zhuh/
Definition: A partial or total loss of memory, typically caused by brain injury, disease, or severe psychological trauma.
Example: "Anterograde amnesia, caused by hippocampal damage, prevents patients from forming any new long-term memories."
Collocations: anterograde amnesia | retrograde amnesia | temporary amnesia

## Section 3: Learning and Conditioning

**learning** *(noun)* — /LUR-ning/
Definition: A relatively permanent change in knowledge, behaviour, or skills resulting from experience or practice.
Example: "Spaced repetition is one of the most well-supported strategies for accelerating the learning of new vocabulary."
Collocations: active learning | deep learning | learning outcome

**conditioning** *(noun)* — /kun-DISH-un-ing/
Definition: A form of learning in which an organism's behaviour is modified through associations with stimuli or consequences.
Example: "Conditioning experiments with animals helped psychologists develop early theories of human behaviour change."
Collocations: classical conditioning | operant conditioning | fear conditioning

**classical conditioning** *(noun phrase)* — /KLAS-ih-kul kun-DISH-un-ing/
Definition: A learning process in which a neutral stimulus becomes associated with a meaningful stimulus and triggers a similar response.
Example: "Pavlov's famous experiment demonstrated classical conditioning by training dogs to salivate at the sound of a bell."
Collocations: classical conditioning experiment | classical conditioning principles | apply classical conditioning

**operant conditioning** *(noun phrase)* — /OP-ur-unt kun-DISH-un-ing/
Definition: A learning method in which behaviour is strengthened or weakened by its consequences, such as rewards or punishments.
Example: "Operant conditioning is widely used in schools, where praise and grades reinforce students' academic efforts."
Collocations: operant conditioning principles | operant conditioning chamber | operant conditioning therapy

**reinforcement** *(noun)* — /ree-in-FORS-munt/
Definition: Any consequence that increases the likelihood that a behaviour will be repeated in the future.
Example: "Consistent reinforcement of desired behaviours is more effective than irregular or unpredictable reward schedules."
Collocations: positive reinforcement | negative reinforcement | schedule of reinforcement

**positive reinforcement** *(noun phrase)* — /POZ-ih-tiv ree-in-FORS-munt/
Definition: The addition of a pleasant stimulus following a behaviour, which increases the probability of that behaviour recurring.
Example: "Giving a child praise immediately after tidying their room is an example of positive reinforcement."
Collocations: apply positive reinforcement | positive reinforcement strategy | respond to positive reinforcement

**negative reinforcement** *(noun phrase)* — /NEG-uh-tiv ree-in-FORS-munt/
Definition: The removal of an unpleasant stimulus following a behaviour, which increases the likelihood of that behaviour being repeated.
Example: "Taking painkillers to relieve a headache is a form of negative reinforcement because the relief encourages the same behaviour."
Collocations: negative reinforcement schedule | negative reinforcement loop | distinguish from punishment

**punishment** *(noun)* — /PUN-ish-munt/
Definition: An unpleasant consequence that follows a behaviour and reduces the likelihood of that behaviour occurring again.
Example: "Research indicates that consistent mild punishment is more effective than harsh, irregular punitive measures."
Collocations: positive punishment | negative punishment | corporal punishment

**extinction** *(noun)* — /ek-STINK-shun/
Definition: In psychology, the gradual weakening and disappearance of a conditioned response when reinforcement is withheld.
Example: "Extinction of a fear response can be achieved through repeated exposure to the feared stimulus without any harmful outcome."
Collocations: extinction of behaviour | extinction trial | extinction burst

**stimulus** *(noun)* — /STIM-yoo-lus/
Definition: Any event, object, or circumstance in the environment that triggers a response in an organism.
Example: "The flashing light served as a conditioned stimulus that reliably triggered a fear response in the participants."
Collocations: conditioned stimulus | neutral stimulus | sensory stimulus

**response** *(noun)* — /rih-SPONS/
Definition: A behavioural or physiological reaction to a stimulus.
Example: "The galvanic skin response, measured by changes in skin conductance, is used as a physiological indicator of arousal."
Collocations: conditioned response | unconditioned response | emotional response

**reflex** *(noun)* — /REE-fleks/
Definition: An automatic, involuntary response to a stimulus that occurs without conscious thought.
Example: "The knee-jerk reflex is a standard neurological test used by doctors to assess spinal cord function."
Collocations: reflex action | conditioned reflex | reflex arc

**instinct** *(noun)* — /IN-stinkt/
Definition: An innate, fixed pattern of behaviour that is triggered by specific stimuli without prior learning.
Example: "The maternal instinct observed in many mammals suggests that some caregiving behaviours have a strong biological basis."
Collocations: natural instinct | survival instinct | act on instinct

**social learning** *(noun phrase)* — /SOH-shul LUR-ning/
Definition: Learning that occurs through observing and imitating the behaviours of others in a social context.
Example: "Bandura's Bobo doll experiment provided compelling evidence for social learning theory in children."
Collocations: social learning theory | social learning process | social learning environment

**observational learning** *(noun phrase)* — /ob-zur-VAY-shun-ul LUR-ning/
Definition: A type of learning in which an individual acquires new behaviours by watching others perform them.
Example: "Observational learning explains how children pick up language patterns and social rules from those around them."
Collocations: observational learning model | observational learning effect | acquire through observational learning

**modelling** *(noun)* — /MOD-ul-ing/
Definition: The process of learning a behaviour by observing and imitating a role model.
Example: "In behavioural therapy, modelling is used to demonstrate appropriate social skills to individuals with social anxiety."
Collocations: behavioural modelling | role modelling | modelling effect

**imitation** *(noun)* — /im-ih-TAY-shun/
Definition: The act of copying another person's behaviour, often as a means of social learning.
Example: "Imitation of adult facial expressions by newborns suggests that the capacity for social mimicry is present at birth."
Collocations: deliberate imitation | imitation learning | imitation game

**habit** *(noun)* — /HAB-it/
Definition: A well-established pattern of behaviour that is performed automatically with little conscious thought.
Example: "The formation of a habit requires consistent repetition of a behaviour until it becomes automatic."
Collocations: form a habit | break a habit | habitual behaviour

## Section 4: Motivation and Emotion

**motivation** *(noun)* — /moh-tih-VAY-shun/
Definition: The internal process that initiates, directs, and sustains goal-directed behaviour.
Example: "Intrinsic motivation, driven by personal interest rather than external reward, tends to produce more enduring behaviour change."
Collocations: intrinsic motivation | extrinsic motivation | lack of motivation

**intrinsic** *(adjective)* — /in-TRIN-zik/
Definition: Coming from within a person; relating to internal drives, personal satisfaction, or inherent interest.
Example: "Students with intrinsic motivation tend to engage more deeply with material and achieve better academic outcomes."
Collocations: intrinsic motivation | intrinsic value | intrinsic reward

**extrinsic** *(adjective)* — /eks-TRIN-zik/
Definition: Coming from outside a person; relating to external factors such as money, grades, or social approval.
Example: "Over-reliance on extrinsic rewards can undermine intrinsic motivation, a phenomenon known as the overjustification effect."
Collocations: extrinsic motivation | extrinsic reward | extrinsic factor

**drive** *(noun)* — /DRYV/
Definition: A motivational state arising from an internal need that pushes an organism to seek a goal.
Example: "Hull's drive reduction theory proposed that behaviour is motivated by the need to reduce uncomfortable internal states."
Collocations: biological drive | sex drive | drive reduction

**need** *(noun)* — /NEED/
Definition: A condition of deficiency or lack that motivates an organism to take action to restore balance.
Example: "Maslow argued that basic physiological needs such as food and shelter must be satisfied before higher needs can emerge."
Collocations: basic need | psychological need | unmet need

**hierarchy of needs** *(noun phrase)* — /HY-uh-rar-kee uv NEEDZ/
Definition: Maslow's theoretical model presenting human needs in a ranked order from basic survival to self-fulfilment.
Example: "According to the hierarchy of needs, individuals focus on safety and security before pursuing social connection."
Collocations: Maslow's hierarchy of needs | levels in the hierarchy | top of the hierarchy

**Maslow** *(proper noun)* — /MAZ-loh/
Definition: Abraham Maslow, the American psychologist who proposed the hierarchy of needs theory of human motivation.
Example: "Maslow's influential theory suggests that the drive for self-actualisation emerges only when lower-order needs are met."
Collocations: Maslow's hierarchy | Maslow's theory | Abraham Maslow

**self-actualisation** *(noun)* — /self-ak-choo-ul-ih-ZAY-shun/
Definition: The highest level of Maslow's hierarchy, representing the realisation of one's full potential and deepest values.
Example: "Artists and scientists who devote their lives to creative work are often cited as examples of self-actualisation."
Collocations: achieve self-actualisation | pursuit of self-actualisation | Maslow's self-actualisation

**emotion** *(noun)* — /ih-MOH-shun/
Definition: A complex psychological state involving a subjective feeling, a physiological reaction, and a behavioural response.
Example: "Research in affective neuroscience has revealed the specific brain regions responsible for processing basic emotions."
Collocations: express an emotion | regulate emotion | basic emotion

**affect** *(noun)* — /AF-ekt/
Definition: In psychology, the general term for an observable expression of emotion or feeling.
Example: "Clinicians assessed the patient's affect during the interview, noting that his emotional expression was flat and restricted."
Collocations: positive affect | negative affect | blunted affect

**mood** *(noun)* — /MOOD/
Definition: A relatively sustained emotional state that is less intense and less specific than a discrete emotion.
Example: "Prolonged negative mood that persists for more than two weeks may be an early indicator of clinical depression."
Collocations: improve mood | low mood | mood disorder

**arousal** *(noun)* — /uh-ROW-zul/
Definition: A state of physiological and psychological activation or alertness in response to stimulation.
Example: "The Yerkes-Dodson law describes an inverted-U relationship between arousal and performance on complex tasks."
Collocations: level of arousal | physiological arousal | arousal theory

**self-determination theory** *(noun phrase)* — /self-deh-tur-mih-NAY-shun THEE-uh-ree/
Definition: A theory of motivation proposing that three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — drive intrinsic motivation.
Example: "Self-determination theory predicts that workplaces fostering autonomy will see greater employee engagement and wellbeing."
Collocations: self-determination theory framework | apply self-determination theory | basic needs in self-determination theory

**autonomy** *(noun)* — /aw-TON-uh-mee/
Definition: The experience of having choice and control over one's own actions and decisions.
Example: "Granting students greater autonomy over their learning tasks has been shown to increase intrinsic motivation."
Collocations: sense of autonomy | support autonomy | autonomy-supportive environment

## Section 5: Stress and Mental Health

**stress** *(noun)* — /STRES/
Definition: A state of psychological and physiological tension arising from demands that exceed a person's perceived coping resources.
Example: "Chronic occupational stress has been linked to elevated cortisol levels, impaired immunity, and cardiovascular disease."
Collocations: manage stress | chronic stress | stress response

**anxiety** *(noun)* — /ang-ZY-uh-tee/
Definition: A persistent feeling of worry, nervousness, or unease, typically about an uncertain outcome or anticipated threat.
Example: "Social anxiety disorder can severely limit an individual's ability to participate in everyday academic and professional life."
Collocations: social anxiety | anxiety disorder | anxiety management

**depression** *(noun)* — /dih-PRESH-un/
Definition: A mood disorder characterised by persistent sadness, loss of interest, and impaired daily functioning.
Example: "Clinical depression affects approximately one in five people at some point in their lifetime, making it one of the most prevalent mental health conditions."
Collocations: clinical depression | major depression | treat depression

**phobia** *(noun)* — /FOH-bee-uh/
Definition: An intense, irrational, and persistent fear of a specific object, situation, or activity.
Example: "Specific phobias, such as arachnophobia, are among the most common anxiety disorders treated in clinical practice."
Collocations: specific phobia | social phobia | develop a phobia

**trauma** *(noun)* — /TRAW-muh/
Definition: A deeply distressing or disturbing experience that overwhelms an individual's ability to cope.
Example: "Childhood trauma can have lasting effects on brain development, particularly in regions associated with fear and stress responses."
Collocations: psychological trauma | childhood trauma | trauma response

**post-traumatic** *(adjective)* — /pohst-traw-MAT-ik/
Definition: Relating to the psychological effects that occur after a person has experienced a traumatic event.
Example: "Post-traumatic stress disorder is diagnosed when intrusive symptoms persist for more than one month after a traumatic event."
Collocations: post-traumatic stress | post-traumatic growth | post-traumatic symptoms

**resilience** *(noun)* — /rih-ZIL-ee-unts/
Definition: The capacity to recover quickly from difficulties or to adapt positively in the face of significant adversity.
Example: "Psychologists have identified several factors that promote resilience, including strong social support networks and positive self-belief."
Collocations: psychological resilience | build resilience | resilience factor

**coping** *(noun)* — /KOH-ping/
Definition: The cognitive and behavioural strategies used to manage the internal and external demands of stressful situations.
Example: "Problem-focused coping strategies, such as planning and seeking information, tend to be effective when challenges are controllable."
Collocations: coping strategy | coping mechanism | effective coping

**defence mechanism** *(noun phrase)* — /dih-FENS MEK-uh-niz-um/
Definition: In psychoanalytic theory, an unconscious mental strategy used to protect the self from anxiety or unacceptable thoughts.
Example: "Defence mechanisms such as repression and denial were described by Freud as unconscious ways of managing psychological conflict."
Collocations: psychological defence mechanism | unconscious defence mechanism | deploy a defence mechanism

**repression** *(noun)* — /rih-PRESH-un/
Definition: A defence mechanism in which distressing thoughts, memories, or impulses are pushed out of conscious awareness.
Example: "Freud proposed that repression of traumatic memories could lead to neurotic symptoms in adult life."
Collocations: psychic repression | repression of memory | mechanism of repression

**denial** *(noun)* — /dih-NY-ul/
Definition: A defence mechanism involving the refusal to accept a painful or threatening reality.
Example: "Denial is commonly observed in individuals who have recently received a serious medical diagnosis."
Collocations: state of denial | denial as a defence | cognitive denial

**projection** *(noun)* — /pruh-JEK-shun/
Definition: A defence mechanism in which a person attributes their own unacceptable thoughts or feelings to others.
Example: "A person who feels hostility towards a colleague may use projection and instead accuse the colleague of being hostile."
Collocations: psychological projection | projection defence | project feelings onto others

**rationalisation** *(noun)* — /rash-un-ul-ih-ZAY-shun/
Definition: A defence mechanism in which a person constructs logical-sounding justifications for unacceptable behaviour or feelings.
Example: "Students sometimes use rationalisation, telling themselves they failed due to an unfair test rather than insufficient preparation."
Collocations: cognitive rationalisation | post-hoc rationalisation | use rationalisation as a defence

**displacement** *(noun)* — /dis-PLAYS-munt/
Definition: A defence mechanism in which emotions directed at one target are redirected towards a safer or more acceptable target.
Example: "A worker who feels anger towards their employer may use displacement and direct that frustration at family members."
Collocations: emotional displacement | displacement of aggression | displacement activity

**sublimation** *(noun)* — /sub-lih-MAY-shun/
Definition: A mature defence mechanism in which socially unacceptable impulses are channelled into constructive or socially valued activities.
Example: "Sublimation may explain why some artists channel intense personal suffering into highly creative and productive work."
Collocations: Freudian sublimation | sublimation of impulse | sublimation as a defence

**burnout** *(noun)* — /BURN-owt/
Definition: A state of chronic exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced professional efficacy caused by prolonged workplace stress.
Example: "Healthcare workers are at elevated risk of burnout due to the emotional demands of caring for patients over long periods."
Collocations: professional burnout | prevent burnout | signs of burnout

**learned helplessness** *(noun phrase)* — /LURND HELP-lus-nes/
Definition: A condition in which a person believes they have no control over their situation after repeated exposure to uncontrollable events.
Example: "Seligman's experiments with dogs demonstrated learned helplessness, revealing how prior inescapable stress reduces future coping."
Collocations: develop learned helplessness | overcome learned helplessness | learned helplessness model

**wellbeing** *(noun)* — /WEL-bee-ing/
Definition: A positive state of health, happiness, and fulfilment across physical, psychological, and social dimensions.
Example: "Positive psychology focuses on enhancing subjective wellbeing by building on individuals' strengths rather than treating deficits."
Collocations: psychological wellbeing | subjective wellbeing | promote wellbeing

## Section 6: Personality

**personality** *(noun)* — /pur-sun-AL-ih-tee/
Definition: The stable, enduring pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviours that distinguishes one person from another.
Example: "Personality is thought to be shaped by both genetic predispositions and environmental influences throughout development."
Collocations: personality trait | personality disorder | personality type

**trait** *(noun)* — /TRAYT/
Definition: A relatively stable and consistent characteristic of a person that influences their behaviour across different situations.
Example: "The trait of conscientiousness has been reliably linked to academic achievement and long-term career success."
Collocations: personality trait | character trait | trait theory

**introvert** *(noun/adjective)* — /IN-truh-vurt/

Example: "The misconception that introverts are antisocial fails to recognise that they simply require more time alone to restore their energy."
Collocations: introvert personality | introvert-extrovert scale | identify as an introvert

**extrovert** *(noun/adjective)* — /EK-struh-vurt/
Definition: A person who gains energy from social interaction and tends to be outgoing, talkative, and action-oriented.

Collocations: extrovert personality | introvert–extrovert dimension | typical extrovert

**agreeableness** *(noun)* — /uh-GREE-uh-bul-nes/

Collocations: high agreeableness | agreeableness scale | low agreeableness

**conscientiousness** *(noun)* — /kon-shee-EN-shus-nes/
Definition: One of the Big Five traits, characterised by organisation, self-discipline, and a strong sense of duty.

Collocations: high conscientiousness | measure conscientiousness | conscientiousness score

**neuroticism** *(noun)* — /nyoo-ROT-ih-siz-um/

**openness** *(noun)* — /OH-pun-nes/

Collocations: openness to experience | high openness | openness dimension

**Big Five** *(noun phrase)* — /BIG FYV/

Collocations: Big Five model | Big Five traits | Big Five assessment

**temperament** *(noun)* — /TEM-pur-uh-munt/

Collocations: infant temperament | difficult temperament | temperament type

**character** *(noun)* — /KAR-ik-tur/
Definition: The moral and ethical qualities of a person that are shaped by experience and values.
Example: "Many educators argue that schooling should develop character as well as academic knowledge."
Collocations: moral character | character development | character trait

**identity** *(noun)* — /y-DEN-tih-tee/
Definition: The stable sense of who one is, including values, beliefs, roles, and group memberships.
Example: "Adolescence is a critical period for identity formation, during which individuals explore different roles and values."
Collocations: personal identity | social identity | identity crisis
