# Vocabulary: Business & Economics

**Word count:** 320
**Study method:** Read 20 words per session. For each word: read the definition, cover it, say the definition from memory, then say the example sentence aloud using British RP.

---

**economics** *(noun)* — /ee-kuh-NOM-iks/
Definition: The social science that studies how societies allocate scarce resources to satisfy unlimited wants.
Example: "Students who study economics learn to analyse how governments and households make decisions under conditions of scarcity."
Collocations: study economics | applied economics | behavioural economics

**economic** *(adjective)* — /ee-kuh-NOM-ik/
Definition: Relating to the economy, trade, or finance of a country or region.
Example: "The government introduced a series of economic reforms designed to attract foreign investment."
Collocations: economic growth | economic policy | economic recession

**economy** *(noun)* — /ih-KON-uh-mee/
Definition: The system by which a country's money, trade, and industry are organised and managed.
Example: "A diversified economy is generally more resilient to external shocks than one that relies on a single export commodity."
Collocations: market economy | domestic economy | stimulate the economy

**microeconomics** *(noun)* — /MY-kroh-ee-kuh-NOM-iks/
Definition: The branch of economics concerned with the behaviour of individual consumers, firms, and markets.
Example: "Microeconomics helps explain why consumers buy less of a product when its price rises, all else being equal."
Collocations: microeconomic theory | microeconomic analysis | microeconomic model

**macroeconomics** *(noun)* — /MAK-roh-ee-kuh-NOM-iks/
Definition: The branch of economics that studies the performance and structure of entire national or global economies.
Example: "Macroeconomics examines how fiscal and monetary policies interact to influence aggregate output and employment."
Collocations: macroeconomic policy | macroeconomic indicator | macroeconomic stability

**supply** *(noun)* — /suh-PLY/
Definition: The total quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices.
Example: "A drought in the coffee-growing regions caused a sharp fall in supply, which drove up prices worldwide."
Collocations: supply curve | supply shock | aggregate supply

**demand** *(noun)* — /dih-MAND/
Definition: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a given period.
Example: "Rising incomes in emerging markets have generated strong demand for consumer electronics."
Collocations: demand curve | aggregate demand | demand-side policy

**equilibrium** *(noun)* — /ee-kwuh-LIB-ree-um/
Definition: The state in which market supply and demand are balanced so that prices remain stable.
Example: "In a competitive market, prices tend to move towards equilibrium where the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded."
Collocations: market equilibrium | equilibrium price | Nash equilibrium

**market** *(noun)* — /MAR-kit/
Definition: Any system or arrangement through which buyers and sellers exchange goods, services, or financial assets.
Example: "The housing market in that city overheated when low interest rates encouraged excessive borrowing."
Collocations: market forces | emerging market | labour market

**price** *(noun)* — /PRYS/
Definition: The amount of money for which a good or service is bought or sold.
Example: "When the price of oil rises significantly, transportation costs throughout the supply chain tend to increase."
Collocations: price level | price mechanism | price ceiling

**inflation** *(noun)* — /in-FLAY-shun/
Definition: A sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services, reducing the purchasing power of money.
Example: "Central banks typically raise interest rates to curb inflation when it rises above their target level."
Collocations: inflation rate | combat inflation | inflationary pressure

**deflation** *(noun)* — /dih-FLAY-shun/
Definition: A sustained decrease in the general price level, often associated with reduced economic activity and consumer spending.
Example: "Japan experienced prolonged deflation in the 1990s, which discouraged investment and consumer spending."
Collocations: deflationary spiral | deflationary pressure | combat deflation

**stagflation** *(noun)* — /stag-FLAY-shun/
Definition: A rare economic condition characterised by simultaneously high inflation, high unemployment, and slow economic growth.
Example: "The 1970s oil crisis triggered stagflation in many Western economies, challenging traditional Keynesian policy prescriptions."
Collocations: period of stagflation | stagflation crisis | stagflationary environment

**recession** *(noun)* — /rih-SESH-un/
Definition: A period of temporary economic decline, typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.
Example: "The global financial crisis of 2008 triggered a severe recession that led to millions of job losses."
Collocations: economic recession | fall into recession | recession indicator

**depression** *(noun)* — /dih-PRESH-un/
Definition: A prolonged and severe downturn in economic activity, deeper and longer-lasting than a recession.
Example: "The Great Depression of the 1930s saw unemployment rise above 25 percent in the United States."
Collocations: economic depression | the Great Depression | depression-era policy

**growth** *(noun)* — /GROHTH/
Definition: An increase in the output of goods and services in an economy over time, typically measured as a change in GDP.
Example: "Sustained economic growth requires investment in infrastructure, education, and technological innovation."
Collocations: economic growth | GDP growth | growth rate

**GDP** *(noun)* — /JEE-dee-PEE/
Definition: Gross Domestic Product; the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country in a given period.
Example: "GDP per capita is widely used as a proxy for living standards, though it does not capture income distribution."
Collocations: GDP growth | nominal GDP | GDP per capita

**GNP** *(noun)* — /JEE-en-PEE/
Definition: Gross National Product; the total value of goods and services produced by a country's residents, regardless of location.
Example: "GNP differs from GDP in that it includes income earned abroad by a country's citizens and excludes income earned domestically by foreigners."
Collocations: GNP per capita | national GNP | GNP growth rate

**productivity** *(noun)* — /proh-duk-TIV-ih-tee/
Definition: The efficiency with which inputs such as labour and capital are converted into outputs of goods or services.
Example: "Investing in worker training and modern technology is one of the most effective ways to raise productivity."
Collocations: labour productivity | productivity growth | total factor productivity

**efficiency** *(noun)* — /ih-FISH-en-see/
Definition: The ability to achieve maximum output with minimum waste of resources, time, or effort.
Example: "Allocative efficiency occurs when resources are distributed in a way that maximises consumer and producer welfare."
Collocations: economic efficiency | allocative efficiency | productive efficiency

**allocation** *(noun)* — /al-oh-KAY-shun/
Definition: The distribution of scarce resources among competing uses or users.
Example: "One of the central questions in economics is how societies should determine the optimal allocation of resources."
Collocations: resource allocation | allocate funds | efficient allocation

**scarcity** *(noun)* — /SKAYR-sih-tee/
Definition: The fundamental economic problem that arises because human wants are unlimited but resources to satisfy them are finite.
Example: "Scarcity forces individuals, firms, and governments to make choices about how to use limited resources."
Collocations: resource scarcity | water scarcity | scarcity of labour

**opportunity cost** *(noun phrase)* — /op-ur-TYOO-nih-tee KAWST/
Definition: The value of the next-best alternative that must be forgone when making an economic choice.
Example: "The opportunity cost of attending university full-time includes not only tuition fees but also the wages one could have earned."
Collocations: calculate opportunity cost | opportunity cost of capital | implicit opportunity cost

**trade-off** *(noun)* — /TRAYD-awf/
Definition: A situation in which gaining one thing requires sacrificing or reducing something else of value.
Example: "There is often a trade-off between economic growth and environmental sustainability that policymakers must navigate."
Collocations: face a trade-off | trade-off between X and Y | inherent trade-off

**marginal** *(adjective)* — /MAR-jih-nul/
Definition: Relating to a small, incremental change in quantity, cost, or benefit.
Example: "Firms maximise profit when marginal cost equals marginal revenue, a fundamental principle of microeconomic theory."
Collocations: marginal cost | marginal benefit | marginal utility

**diminishing returns** *(noun phrase)* — /dih-MIN-ish-ing rih-TURNZ/
Definition: The principle that as more of a variable input is added to fixed inputs, the additional output eventually declines.
Example: "A factory that hires additional workers will eventually encounter diminishing returns as the production floor becomes overcrowded."
Collocations: law of diminishing returns | diminishing marginal returns | point of diminishing returns

**elasticity** *(noun)* — /ih-las-TIS-ih-tee/
Definition: A measure of how responsive the quantity demanded or supplied of a good is to a change in price or income.
Example: "The price elasticity of demand for petrol tends to be low in the short run because drivers have few immediate alternatives."
Collocations: price elasticity | income elasticity | cross elasticity of demand

**inelastic** *(adjective)* — /in-ih-LAS-tik/
Definition: Describing a good or service for which demand or supply does not change significantly when price changes.
Example: "Demand for insulin is highly inelastic because diabetic patients have no substitute and must purchase it regardless of price."
Collocations: inelastic demand | price inelastic | relatively inelastic

**elastic** *(adjective)* — /ih-LAS-tik/
Definition: Describing a good or service for which demand or supply changes significantly in response to a price change.
Example: "Luxury holidays are generally considered to have elastic demand, since consumers readily cut back when prices rise."
Collocations: elastic demand | highly elastic | price elastic supply

**substitute** *(noun)* — /SUB-stih-tyoot/
Definition: A good that can replace another in consumption, so that an increase in the price of one raises demand for the other.
Example: "Tea and coffee are classic substitutes; when coffee prices rise sharply, many consumers switch to tea."
Collocations: close substitute | substitute good | substitute product

**complement** *(noun)* — /KOM-pluh-ment/
Definition: A good that is consumed together with another, so that demand for both tends to rise or fall together.
Example: "Printers and ink cartridges are complements; a fall in the price of printers tends to increase demand for cartridges."
Collocations: complementary good | close complement | complement product

**consumer** *(noun)* — /kun-SYOO-mur/
Definition: An individual or household that purchases goods and services for personal use rather than for resale.
Example: "Consumer confidence fell sharply after the announcement of rising unemployment, leading to a decline in retail spending."
Collocations: consumer spending | consumer confidence | consumer behaviour

**producer** *(noun)* — /pruh-DYOO-sur/
Definition: An individual, firm, or organisation that creates goods or services for sale in a market.
Example: "Oil producers carefully monitor global demand forecasts before deciding how much to invest in new extraction capacity."
Collocations: producer surplus | primary producer | producer price index

**firm** *(noun)* — /FURM/
Definition: A business organisation that combines inputs to produce goods or services for profit.
Example: "Firms in a perfectly competitive market are price-takers because they lack the power to influence the market price."
Collocations: private firm | state-owned firm | firm-level productivity

**industry** *(noun)* — /IN-dus-tree/
Definition: A group of firms that produce similar or related goods and services within an economy.
Example: "The automobile industry has faced significant disruption from electric vehicle technology and shifting consumer preferences."
Collocations: heavy industry | service industry | industry-wide regulation

**sector** *(noun)* — /SEK-tur/
Definition: A distinct part of an economy or financial market grouped by the type of activity, ownership, or product.
Example: "The public sector provides essential services such as healthcare and education that private markets may underprovide."
Collocations: public sector | private sector | financial sector

**monopoly** *(noun)* — /muh-NOP-uh-lee/
Definition: A market structure in which a single seller dominates the supply of a good or service with no close substitutes.
Example: "A natural monopoly arises when high infrastructure costs make it most efficient for a single firm to serve the entire market."
Collocations: natural monopoly | monopoly power | break up a monopoly

**oligopoly** *(noun)* — /ol-ih-GOP-uh-lee/
Definition: A market dominated by a small number of large firms that have significant pricing power and strategic interdependence.
Example: "The smartphone industry has the characteristics of an oligopoly, with a few dominant firms controlling the majority of global sales."
Collocations: oligopolistic market | oligopoly behaviour | tacit collusion in oligopoly

**duopoly** *(noun)* — /dyoo-OP-uh-lee/
Definition: A market structure in which exactly two firms dominate supply, each significantly influencing the other's pricing decisions.
Example: "The commercial aircraft industry is often described as a duopoly, with Boeing and Airbus accounting for most global orders."
Collocations: effective duopoly | duopolistic competition | duopoly market

**competition** *(noun)* — /kom-puh-TISH-un/
Definition: The rivalry among sellers trying to achieve such goals as increasing profits, market share, and sales volume.
Example: "Vigorous competition between mobile phone operators resulted in lower prices and better service quality for consumers."
Collocations: market competition | competitive pressure | promote competition

**perfect competition** *(noun phrase)* — /PUR-fekt kom-puh-TISH-un/
Definition: A theoretical market structure with many buyers and sellers, identical products, free entry and exit, and perfect information.
Example: "Perfect competition is rarely observed in practice, but it serves as a useful benchmark for evaluating real market outcomes."
Collocations: model of perfect competition | perfectly competitive market | conditions for perfect competition

**imperfect competition** *(noun phrase)* — /im-PUR-fekt kom-puh-TISH-un/
Definition: Any market structure that deviates from perfect competition, including monopoly, oligopoly, and monopolistic competition.
Example: "Most real-world markets exhibit imperfect competition, where firms have some degree of pricing power over their products."
Collocations: imperfectly competitive market | degree of imperfect competition | analyse imperfect competition

**market failure** *(noun phrase)* — /MAR-kit FAYL-yur/
Definition: A situation in which the free market allocates resources inefficiently, often justifying government intervention.
Example: "Pollution is a classic example of market failure because firms do not bear the full social cost of their emissions."
Collocations: address market failure | cause of market failure | correct market failure

**externality** *(noun)* — /eks-tur-NAL-ih-tee/
Definition: A cost or benefit that affects parties not directly involved in an economic transaction.
Example: "Carbon emissions create a negative externality because the environmental damage is borne by society rather than the polluting firm."
Collocations: negative externality | positive externality | internalise an externality

**public good** *(noun phrase)* — /PUB-lik GOOD/
Definition: A good that is non-excludable and non-rival, meaning it can be consumed by all without reducing availability to others.
Example: "National defence is a classic public good because protecting one citizen does not diminish protection available to others."
Collocations: pure public good | provide public goods | public good problem

**merit good** *(noun phrase)* — /MER-it GOOD/
Definition: A good that society values and believes should be provided to all regardless of ability to pay, often subsidised by government.
Example: "Education is typically treated as a merit good because of its positive spillover effects on the wider economy."
Collocations: subsidise merit goods | merit good provision | identify merit goods

**demerit good** *(noun phrase)* — /DEE-mer-it GOOD/
Definition: A good whose consumption is judged to be harmful to individuals or society, often taxed or restricted by government.
Example: "Tobacco is a demerit good; governments tax it heavily to discourage consumption and offset healthcare costs."
Collocations: tax demerit goods | demerit good regulation | restrict demerit goods

**free rider** *(noun phrase)* — /FREE RY-dur/
Definition: A person or entity that benefits from a public good or collective action without contributing to its cost.
Example: "The free rider problem makes it difficult to finance public goods through voluntary contributions alone."
Collocations: free rider problem | act as a free rider | overcome free riding

**tragedy of the commons** *(noun phrase)* — /TRAJ-ih-dee uv thuh KOM-unz/
Definition: The overexploitation of a shared resource when individual users act in their own self-interest, depleting it for all.
Example: "Overfishing in international waters illustrates the tragedy of the commons when property rights are poorly defined."
Collocations: classic tragedy of the commons | avoid the tragedy of the commons | shared resource tragedy

**regulation** *(noun)* — /reg-yuh-LAY-shun/
Definition: Rules or laws imposed by a government body to control or direct the behaviour of firms and markets.
Example: "Financial regulation was significantly tightened after the 2008 crisis to prevent the excessive risk-taking that had destabilised banks."
Collocations: government regulation | regulatory framework | introduce regulation

**deregulation** *(noun)* — /dee-reg-yuh-LAY-shun/
Definition: The removal or reduction of government rules controlling an industry, intended to increase competition and efficiency.
Example: "Deregulation of the airline industry in the United States led to lower fares and greater route choice for passengers."
Collocations: economic deregulation | wave of deregulation | advocate deregulation

**subsidy** *(noun)* — /SUB-sih-dee/
Definition: A financial grant provided by a government to support a business or activity deemed beneficial to the public.
Example: "Agricultural subsidies in many developed countries are criticised for distorting global food markets and harming farmers in developing nations."
Collocations: government subsidy | receive a subsidy | subsidy payment

**tax** *(noun)* — /TAKS/
Definition: A compulsory financial charge levied by a government on individuals or businesses to fund public services.
Example: "Introducing a carbon tax is one policy instrument for discouraging carbon-intensive production and consumption."
Collocations: income tax | indirect tax | tax revenue

**tariff** *(noun)* — /TAR-if/
Definition: A tax imposed by a government on imported or exported goods, typically used to protect domestic industries.
Example: "The newly imposed tariff on imported steel raised costs for domestic manufacturers that relied on steel as a key input."
Collocations: import tariff | protective tariff | tariff barrier

**quota** *(noun)* — /KWOH-tuh/
Definition: A government-imposed limit on the quantity of a good that may be imported or exported during a given period.
Example: "Import quotas restrict the volume of foreign goods entering the domestic market, often benefiting local producers."
Collocations: import quota | production quota | quota restriction

**trade** *(noun)* — /TRAYD/
Definition: The voluntary exchange of goods, services, or capital between individuals, firms, or countries.
Example: "International trade allows countries to specialise in producing goods at which they have a comparative advantage."
Collocations: international trade | trade agreement | bilateral trade

**free trade** *(noun phrase)* — /FREE TRAYD/
Definition: International trade allowed to proceed without tariffs, quotas, or other restrictive government measures.
Example: "Proponents of free trade argue that it raises living standards by allowing each country to specialise according to comparative advantage."
Collocations: free trade agreement | free trade zone | advocate free trade

**protectionism** *(noun)* — /pruh-TEK-shun-iz-um/
Definition: The economic policy of restricting imports through tariffs, quotas, or other barriers to protect domestic industries.
Example: "A rise in protectionism during the 1930s is widely believed to have deepened and prolonged the Great Depression."
Collocations: economic protectionism | resort to protectionism | protectionist measures

**comparative advantage** *(noun phrase)* — /kom-PAR-uh-tiv ad-VAN-tij/
Definition: The ability of a country or producer to make a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country or producer.
Example: "Even if one country is more efficient at producing everything, trade based on comparative advantage can benefit both parties."
Collocations: principle of comparative advantage | exploit comparative advantage | comparative advantage theory

**absolute advantage** *(noun phrase)* — /AB-suh-loot ad-VAN-tij/
Definition: The ability of a country to produce more of a good using the same resources as another country.
Example: "A country with absolute advantage in agriculture can produce crops more efficiently than its trading partners with the same inputs."
Collocations: absolute advantage in production | demonstrate absolute advantage | concept of absolute advantage

**globalisation** *(noun)* — /gloh-bul-ih-ZAY-shun/
Definition: The increasing integration of the world's economies through trade, investment, technology, and the movement of people.
Example: "Globalisation has expanded markets for exporters but has also increased competitive pressures on domestic manufacturers."
Collocations: economic globalisation | effects of globalisation | globalisation of trade

**export** *(noun/verb)* — /EK-sport/ (noun) | /ek-SPORT/ (verb)
Definition: A good, service, or capital that is sold to buyers in another country.
Example: "Germany's economy is heavily dependent on exports, particularly of high-value manufactured goods such as machinery and vehicles."
Collocations: export market | export revenue | boost exports

**import** *(noun/verb)* — /IM-port/ (noun) | /im-PORT/ (verb)
Definition: A good, service, or capital that is purchased from sellers in another country.
Example: "Rising import costs following currency depreciation pushed up consumer prices across the domestic economy."
Collocations: import duty | import substitution | curb imports

**balance of trade** *(noun phrase)* — /BAL-uns uv TRAYD/
Definition: The difference between the monetary value of a country's exports and imports of goods over a specific period.
Example: "A country with persistently large balance of trade deficits may eventually face pressure on its currency."
Collocations: favourable balance of trade | balance of trade deficit | measure the balance of trade

**trade deficit** *(noun phrase)* — /TRAYD DEF-ih-sit/
Definition: A situation in which a country imports more goods and services than it exports, resulting in a negative trade balance.
Example: "The United Kingdom has run a persistent trade deficit in goods, partly offset by a surplus in services such as finance."
Collocations: widening trade deficit | reduce the trade deficit | current account deficit

**trade surplus** *(noun phrase)* — /TRAYD SUR-plus/
Definition: A situation in which a country exports more goods and services than it imports, resulting in a positive trade balance.
Example: "China's large trade surplus with the United States became a source of significant diplomatic tension."
Collocations: persistent trade surplus | trade surplus nation | current account surplus

**exchange rate** *(noun phrase)* — /eks-CHAYNJ RAYT/
Definition: The price at which one country's currency can be exchanged for another country's currency.
Example: "A weakening exchange rate makes exports cheaper for foreign buyers but raises the cost of imported goods."
Collocations: exchange rate fluctuation | fixed exchange rate | floating exchange rate

**currency** *(noun)* — /KUR-un-see/
Definition: A system of money in general use in a particular country, typically issued by the central bank.
Example: "Several developing countries have opted to adopt the US dollar as their currency to achieve exchange rate stability."
Collocations: foreign currency | currency reserve | currency depreciation

**appreciation** *(noun)* — /uh-pree-shee-AY-shun/
Definition: An increase in the value of a currency or asset relative to another currency or benchmark.
Example: "Appreciation of the Japanese yen made Japanese exports more expensive and reduced the competitiveness of its manufacturing sector."
Collocations: currency appreciation | asset appreciation | capital appreciation

**depreciation** *(noun)* — /dih-pree-shee-AY-shun/
Definition: A decrease in the value of a currency relative to other currencies, or a reduction in the book value of an asset over time.
Example: "Currency depreciation following the balance of payments crisis led to higher import prices and a sharp rise in inflation."
Collocations: currency depreciation | asset depreciation | accelerated depreciation

**interest rate** *(noun phrase)* — /IN-trest RAYT/
Definition: The proportion of a loan charged as interest to the borrower, typically expressed as an annual percentage.
Example: "Central banks raise interest rates to cool inflationary pressures by making borrowing more expensive."
Collocations: raise interest rates | real interest rate | base interest rate

**monetary policy** *(noun phrase)* — /MUN-ih-tuh-ree POL-ih-see/
Definition: The use of interest rates and money supply management by a central bank to achieve macroeconomic objectives.
Example: "Expansionary monetary policy involves lowering interest rates to stimulate borrowing, investment, and consumer spending."
Collocations: conduct monetary policy | monetary policy framework | tighten monetary policy

**fiscal policy** *(noun phrase)* — /FIS-kul POL-ih-see/
Definition: The use of government spending and taxation to influence economic activity and achieve macroeconomic goals.
Example: "During a recession, governments often adopt expansionary fiscal policy by increasing public spending or cutting taxes."
Collocations: fiscal policy stance | expansionary fiscal policy | fiscal policy instrument

**central bank** *(noun phrase)* — /SEN-trul BANK/
Definition: The national institution responsible for managing a country's currency, money supply, and interest rates.
Example: "The central bank announced an emergency cut in interest rates in response to deteriorating economic conditions."
Collocations: central bank policy | central bank independence | central bank intervention

**quantitative easing** *(noun phrase)* — /KWON-tih-tay-tiv EE-zing/

Example: "After interest rates reached near zero, the central bank turned to quantitative easing to provide additional stimulus."
Collocations: programme of quantitative easing | unwind quantitative easing | quantitative easing measures

**bond** *(noun)* — /BOND/
Definition: A fixed-income debt security issued by a government or corporation to raise capital, promising regular interest payments and repayment of principal.
Example: "Investors often buy government bonds during periods of economic uncertainty because they are considered low-risk assets."
Collocations: government bond | issue bonds | bond yield

**stock** *(noun)* — /STOK/
Definition: An ownership share in a corporation, entitling the holder to a portion of the company's assets and earnings.
Example: "The company's stock price fell sharply after it reported lower-than-expected quarterly profits."
Collocations: stock market | stock price | common stock

**share** *(noun)* — /SHAYR/
Definition: A unit of ownership in a company, representing a claim on the company's assets and earnings.
Example: "The IPO allowed the public to purchase shares in the company for the first time, raising significant capital."
Collocations: share price | issue shares | market share

**equity** *(noun)* — /EK-wih-tee/

Collocations: equity market | private equity | equity stake

**dividend** *(noun)* — /DIV-ih-dend/

Collocations: pay a dividend | dividend yield | dividend policy

**portfolio** *(noun)* — /port-FOH-lee-oh/

Collocations: investment portfolio | diversified portfolio | portfolio management

**investment** *(noun)* — /in-VEST-ment/

Collocations: foreign investment | return on investment | capital investment

**capital** *(noun)* — /KAP-ih-tul/

**venture capital** *(noun phrase)* — /VEN-chur KAP-ih-tul/

**private equity** *(noun phrase)* — /PRY-vit EK-wih-tee/

**IPO** *(noun)* — /EYE-pee-OH/

**merger** *(noun)* — /MUR-jur/

**acquisition** *(noun)* — /ak-wih-ZI-shun/

**takeover** *(noun)* — /TAYK-oh-vur/

**conglomerate** *(noun)* — /kun-GLOM-ur-it/

**multinational** *(noun/adjective)* — /mul-tee-NASH-un-ul/

**corporation** *(noun)* — /kor-puh-RAY-shun/

**LLC** *(noun)* — /EL-el-SEE/

**partnership** *(noun)* — /PART-nur-ship/
