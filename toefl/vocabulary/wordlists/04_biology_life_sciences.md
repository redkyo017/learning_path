## Section 1 — Foundations of Biology (001–010)

**biology** *(noun)* — /by-OL-uh-jee/ (OL stressed)
Definition: the scientific study of living organisms and the processes that sustain life.
Example: "Advances in molecular biology have revolutionised our understanding of how genes control cell function."
Collocations: study of biology | biology laboratory | applied biology

**biological** *(adjective)* — /by-uh-LOJ-ih-kul/ (LOJ stressed)
Definition: relating to or affecting living organisms and their natural processes.
Example: "The biological diversity of tropical rainforests makes them critical subjects for conservation research."
Collocations: biological process | biological clock | biological diversity

**organism** *(noun)* — /OR-guh-niz-um/ (OR stressed)
Definition: any living entity capable of growth, reproduction, and response to its environment.
Example: "Single-celled organisms such as Amoeba proteus are classified as protists and thrive in freshwater environments."
Collocations: living organism | multicellular organism | pathogenic organism

**cell** *(noun)* — /sel/ (monosyllabic)
Definition: the smallest structural and functional unit of life, enclosed by a membrane and capable of independent biochemical activity.
Example: "Every human body contains approximately 37 trillion cells, each specialised to perform a specific function."
Collocations: cell membrane | cell division | cell function

**cellular** *(adjective)* — /SEL-yuh-lur/ (SEL stressed)
Definition: relating to, consisting of, or occurring at the level of individual cells.
Example: "Cellular respiration converts glucose into ATP, the primary energy currency used to power biological reactions."
Collocations: cellular respiration | cellular structure | cellular metabolism

**morphology** *(noun)* — /mor-FOL-uh-jee/ (FOL stressed)
Definition: the branch of biology concerned with the form, shape, and external structure of organisms.
Example: "Comparative morphology revealed striking similarities between the forelimbs of whales and bats, suggesting a common ancestor."
Collocations: plant morphology | comparative morphology | morphological feature

**anatomy** *(noun)* — /uh-NAT-uh-mee/ (NAT stressed)
Definition: the scientific study of the internal structure and organisation of the body of an organism.
Example: "Dissection remains an important tool in anatomy courses, allowing students to observe the arrangement of organs directly."
Collocations: human anatomy | study anatomy | anatomical structure

**physiology** *(noun)* — /fiz-ee-OL-uh-jee/ (OL stressed)
Definition: the branch of biology dealing with the normal functions and processes of living organisms or their parts.
Example: "Exercise physiology examines how the cardiovascular and muscular systems respond to sustained physical activity."
Collocations: animal physiology | plant physiology | physiological function

**metabolism** *(noun)* — /muh-TAB-uh-liz-um/ (TAB stressed)
Definition: the complete set of chemical reactions that occur within a living organism to maintain life, including both building and breaking-down processes.
Example: "A high metabolic rate allows small mammals such as shrews to sustain constant body temperature in cold climates."
Collocations: basal metabolism | metabolic rate | metabolic pathway

**taxonomy** *(noun)* — /tak-SON-uh-mee/ (SON stressed)
Definition: the science of naming, describing, and classifying organisms into hierarchical groups based on shared characteristics.
Example: "Modern taxonomy increasingly relies on DNA sequencing rather than purely physical characteristics to establish evolutionary relationships."
Collocations: biological taxonomy | taxonomic classification | taxonomic rank

## Section 2 — Cell Structure & Organelles (011–035)

**prokaryote** *(noun)* — /proh-KAR-ee-oht/ (KAR stressed)
Definition: a single-celled organism whose genetic material is not enclosed within a membrane-bound nucleus.
Example: "Bacteria are prokaryotes whose rapid reproduction makes them both useful in biotechnology and dangerous as pathogens."
Collocations: prokaryotic cell | prokaryotic organism | prokaryote vs. eukaryote

**eukaryote** *(noun)* — /yoo-KAR-ee-oht/ (KAR stressed)
Definition: an organism whose cells contain a membrane-bound nucleus and other membrane-enclosed organelles.
Example: "All animals, plants, and fungi are eukaryotes, distinguished from bacteria by the presence of a true nucleus."
Collocations: eukaryotic cell | eukaryotic organism | eukaryotic evolution

**nucleus** *(noun)* — /NYOO-klee-us/ (NYOO stressed)
Definition: the membrane-enclosed organelle within a eukaryotic cell that contains the cell's genetic material and directs cellular activities.
Example: "The nucleus acts as the control centre of the cell, housing the DNA that encodes all proteins needed for survival."
Collocations: cell nucleus | nuclear membrane | nucleus function

**cytoplasm** *(noun)* — /SY-toh-plaz-um/ (SY stressed)
Definition: the jelly-like fluid and all the contents enclosed within a cell membrane, excluding the nucleus.
Example: "Ribosomes are scattered throughout the cytoplasm where they carry out the translation of messenger RNA into protein."
Collocations: cytoplasmic content | cytoplasmic streaming | cytoplasm organelle

**membrane** *(noun)* — /MEM-brayn/ (MEM stressed)
Definition: a thin, flexible layer of lipids and proteins that encloses a cell or organelle and regulates the passage of substances.
Example: "The plasma membrane is selectively permeable, allowing water to pass freely while restricting the movement of larger molecules."
Collocations: cell membrane | plasma membrane | membrane permeability

**mitochondria** *(noun, plural)* — /my-toh-KON-dree-uh/ (KON stressed)
Definition: organelles found in eukaryotic cells that generate ATP through cellular respiration, often described as the powerhouses of the cell.
Example: "Mitochondria contain their own DNA, supporting the endosymbiotic theory that they were once free-living bacteria."
Collocations: mitochondrial DNA | mitochondrial function | mitochondria ATP production

**chloroplast** *(noun)* — /KLOR-oh-plast/ (KLOR stressed)
Definition: a plant cell organelle that contains chlorophyll and carries out photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy.
Example: "The high density of chloroplasts in leaf mesophyll cells maximises the capture of sunlight for photosynthesis."
Collocations: chloroplast membrane | chloroplast function | chloroplast structure

**ribosome** *(noun)* — /RY-boh-sohm/ (RY stressed)
Definition: a small cellular organelle, found in all living cells, that assembles proteins by reading messenger RNA sequences.
Example: "Free ribosomes in the cytoplasm synthesise proteins used within the cell, while those on the rough endoplasmic reticulum produce proteins destined for export."
Collocations: ribosomal RNA | ribosome synthesis | ribosome structure

**organelle** *(noun)* — /or-guh-NEL/ (NEL stressed)
Definition: a specialised subunit within a cell that performs a specific function, analogous to an organ within the body.
Example: "Each organelle is enclosed by one or more membranes that separate its chemical environment from the rest of the cytoplasm."
Collocations: membrane-bound organelle | organelle function | cellular organelle

**cytoskeleton** *(noun)* — /sy-toh-SKEL-uh-ton/ (SKEL stressed)
Definition: a network of protein fibres within the cytoplasm of a cell that provides structural support and enables cell movement.
Example: "The cytoskeleton plays an essential role in cell division by forming the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes."
Collocations: cytoskeletal fibre | cytoskeleton function | actin cytoskeleton

**vacuole** *(noun)* — /VAK-yoo-ohl/ (VAK stressed)
Definition: a membrane-bound space within the cytoplasm that stores water, nutrients, or waste products.
Example: "The large central vacuole in plant cells helps maintain turgor pressure, keeping the plant upright and rigid."
Collocations: central vacuole | vacuole function | vacuole membrane

**endoplasmic reticulum** *(noun)* — /en-doh-PLAZ-mik reh-TIK-yoo-lum/ (PLAZ and TIK stressed)
Definition: an extensive network of membranes within the cytoplasm involved in the synthesis, folding, and transport of proteins and lipids.
Example: "The rough endoplasmic reticulum is studded with ribosomes that synthesise proteins destined for secretion or the cell membrane."
Collocations: rough endoplasmic reticulum | smooth endoplasmic reticulum | endoplasmic reticulum function

**Golgi apparatus** *(noun)* — /GOL-jee ap-uh-RAY-tus/ (GOL and RAY stressed)
Definition: a stack of membrane-bound sacs in the cell that modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for secretion or internal use.
Example: "After synthesis on the endoplasmic reticulum, proteins are transported to the Golgi apparatus for final processing and distribution."
Collocations: Golgi body | Golgi function | Golgi membrane

**lysosome** *(noun)* — /LY-soh-sohm/ (LY stressed)
Definition: an organelle containing digestive enzymes that break down waste materials, cellular debris, and foreign particles within the cell.
Example: "When a cell engulfs a pathogen by phagocytosis, lysosomes fuse with the resulting vesicle to destroy the invader."
Collocations: lysosomal enzyme | lysosome function | lysosomal digestion

**vesicle** *(noun)* — /VES-ih-kul/ (VES stressed)
Definition: a small membrane-enclosed sac that transports substances within or between cells.
Example: "Vesicles bud off from the Golgi apparatus carrying proteins to the cell surface for secretion into the extracellular space."
Collocations: transport vesicle | vesicle fusion | secretory vesicle

**cell wall** *(noun)* — /sel wol/ (monosyllabic compound)
Definition: a rigid outer layer surrounding the plasma membrane of plant, fungal, and bacterial cells that provides structural support and protection.
Example: "The cellulose cell wall of plant cells prevents excessive water uptake during osmosis and maintains cell shape under turgor pressure."
Collocations: plant cell wall | cell wall synthesis | cellulose cell wall

**flagellum** *(noun)* — /fluh-JEL-um/ (JEL stressed)
Definition: a whip-like appendage extending from a cell that enables locomotion through a rotating or undulating movement.
Example: "E. coli bacteria use multiple flagella to propel themselves towards chemical attractants in a process called chemotaxis."
Collocations: bacterial flagellum | flagellar rotation | flagellum function

**cilia** *(noun, plural)* — /SIL-ee-uh/ (SIL stressed)
Definition: tiny hair-like projections on the surface of certain cells that beat in coordinated waves to move fluid or propel the cell.
Example: "Cilia lining the respiratory tract sweep mucus and trapped particles away from the lungs towards the throat."
Collocations: ciliary movement | respiratory cilia | ciliated epithelium

**plasma membrane** *(noun)* — /PLAZ-muh MEM-brayn/ (PLAZ and MEM stressed)
Definition: the phospholipid bilayer that forms the outer boundary of a cell and controls the movement of substances in and out.
Example: "Channel proteins embedded in the plasma membrane allow specific ions to pass through without expending cellular energy."
Collocations: plasma membrane function | plasma membrane permeability | plasma membrane structure

## Section 3 — Genetics & Heredity (036–075)

**DNA** *(noun)* — /dee-en-AY/ (AY stressed)
Definition: deoxyribonucleic acid; the double-stranded molecule that carries the genetic instructions for the development and functioning of all living organisms.
Example: "Forensic scientists use DNA fingerprinting to match biological evidence from crime scenes to individuals with near-perfect accuracy."
Collocations: DNA replication | DNA strand | DNA sequence

**RNA** *(noun)* — /ar-en-AY/ (AY stressed)
Definition: ribonucleic acid; a single-stranded molecule that carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes and plays roles in gene expression.
Example: "Messenger RNA carries the genetic code transcribed from DNA in the nucleus to ribosomes in the cytoplasm."
Collocations: messenger RNA | RNA strand | RNA polymerase

**gene** *(noun)* — /jeen/ (monosyllabic)
Definition: a segment of DNA that encodes the information needed to produce a specific functional molecule, usually a protein.
Example: "The discovery of the BRCA1 gene enabled scientists to identify individuals with a significantly elevated risk of developing breast cancer."
Collocations: gene expression | gene mutation | gene therapy

**genetic** *(adjective)* — /juh-NET-ik/ (NET stressed)
Definition: relating to genes, heredity, or the inherited characteristics of an organism.
Example: "Genetic analysis of ancient human remains has revealed unexpected patterns of migration across prehistoric continents."
Collocations: genetic material | genetic variation | genetic disorder

**genome** *(noun)* — /JEE-nohm/ (JEE stressed)
Definition: the complete set of genetic material present in an organism, including all of its genes and non-coding DNA sequences.
Example: "The sequencing of the human genome, completed in 2003, provided a comprehensive map of approximately 20,000 protein-coding genes."
Collocations: genome sequencing | genome mapping | human genome

**genotype** *(noun)* — /JEE-noh-typ/ (JEE stressed)
Definition: the genetic makeup of an organism; the specific combination of alleles it carries for a given trait or across its entire genome.
Example: "Although two plants may have the same phenotype for flower colour, their genotypes may differ if one carries a hidden recessive allele."
Collocations: genotype determination | genotype vs phenotype | genotype frequency

**phenotype** *(noun)* — /FEE-noh-typ/ (FEE stressed)
Definition: the observable physical and biochemical characteristics of an organism, resulting from the interaction of its genotype and environment.
Example: "Height in humans is a phenotype influenced by both genetic factors and environmental variables such as nutrition during development."
Collocations: phenotype expression | phenotypic variation | phenotype vs genotype

**allele** *(noun)* — /uh-LEEL/ (LEEL stressed)
Definition: one of two or more alternative versions of a gene that can occupy the same position on a chromosome and produce different traits.
Example: "The allele for brown eye colour is dominant, so a person carrying one brown and one blue allele will have brown eyes."
Collocations: dominant allele | recessive allele | allele frequency

**dominant** *(adjective)* — /DOM-ih-nunt/ (DOM stressed)
Definition: describing an allele or trait that is expressed in the phenotype whenever it is present, even in a single copy.
Example: "In Mendelian genetics, the dominant allele for tall stem length in pea plants masks the expression of the recessive short-stem allele."
Collocations: dominant trait | dominant allele | dominant gene

**recessive** *(adjective)* — /rih-SES-iv/ (SES stressed)
Definition: describing an allele or trait that is expressed in the phenotype only when two copies of it are present, one from each parent.
Example: "Cystic fibrosis is caused by a recessive allele; a child must inherit two copies of the faulty gene to develop the disease."
Collocations: recessive trait | recessive allele | recessive gene

**mutation** *(noun)* — /myoo-TAY-shun/ (TAY stressed)
Definition: a permanent alteration in the DNA sequence of a gene or chromosome that may affect the structure or function of the encoded protein.
Example: "A point mutation in the haemoglobin gene can cause sickle cell disease, in which red blood cells assume an abnormal crescent shape."
Collocations: genetic mutation | point mutation | mutation rate

**heredity** *(noun)* — /huh-RED-ih-tee/ (RED stressed)
Definition: the biological transmission of genetic traits and characteristics from parents to offspring through reproduction.
Example: "Mendel's experiments with pea plants laid the foundation for our modern understanding of heredity and the laws of inheritance."
Collocations: laws of heredity | heredity and environment | hereditary trait

**inheritance** *(noun)* — /in-HER-ih-tuns/ (HER stressed)
Definition: the process by which genetic information is passed from one generation to the next.
Example: "The inheritance of blood type in humans follows a predictable pattern based on which alleles each parent contributes."
Collocations: genetic inheritance | inheritance pattern | inheritance of traits

**trait** *(noun)* — /trayt/ (monosyllabic)
Definition: a genetically determined characteristic or feature of an organism that can be observed, measured, or described.
Example: "Eye colour, height, and blood type are heritable traits that follow predictable patterns of genetic transmission between generations."
Collocations: heritable trait | physical trait | trait expression

**variation** *(noun)* — /vair-ee-AY-shun/ (AY stressed)
Definition: differences in characteristics between individuals of the same species, arising from genetic differences or environmental influences.
Example: "Genetic variation within a population is essential for natural selection to act upon, driving evolutionary change over time."
Collocations: genetic variation | variation in population | natural variation

**chromosome** *(noun)* — /KROH-moh-sohm/ (KROH stressed)
Definition: a thread-like structure composed of DNA and proteins, found in the nucleus of eukaryotic cells, that carries genetic information.
Example: "Humans possess 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs, with one chromosome of each pair inherited from each parent."
Collocations: chromosome pair | chromosome number | chromosomal abnormality

**base pair** *(noun)* — /BAYS pair/ (BAYS stressed)
Definition: a pair of complementary nucleotide bases (adenine–thymine or cytosine–guanine) held together by hydrogen bonds, forming the rungs of the DNA ladder.
Example: "The human genome contains approximately three billion base pairs, encoding the instructions for building and operating the human body."
Collocations: base pair sequence | DNA base pair | base-pairing rule

**double helix** *(noun)* — /DUB-ul HEE-liks/ (DUB and HEE stressed)
Definition: the twisted ladder-shaped structure of DNA, consisting of two antiparallel strands wound around a common axis.
Example: "Watson and Crick's 1953 model of the double helix revolutionised biology by explaining how genetic information could be faithfully copied."
Collocations: DNA double helix | double-helix structure | helix unwinding

**replication** *(noun)* — /rep-lih-KAY-shun/ (KAY stressed)
Definition: the process by which a DNA molecule is copied to produce two identical copies before cell division.
Example: "DNA replication proceeds in a semi-conservative manner, so each daughter cell receives one original strand and one newly synthesised strand."
Collocations: DNA replication | replication fork | replication error

**intron** *(noun)* — /IN-tron/ (IN stressed)
Definition: a non-coding segment of a gene that is transcribed into RNA but removed by splicing before the RNA is translated into protein.
Example: "Introns were once regarded as junk DNA, but research has revealed that many play regulatory roles in gene expression."
Collocations: intron sequence | intron splicing | intron vs exon

**exon** *(noun)* — /EK-son/ (EK stressed)
Definition: a coding segment of a gene that is retained in the mature messenger RNA and translated into a protein.
Example: "Alternative splicing of exons from a single gene can produce multiple different proteins, greatly expanding proteome diversity."
Collocations: exon sequence | exon splicing | exon skipping

**epigenetics** *(noun)* — /ep-ih-juh-NET-iks/ (NET stressed)
Definition: the study of heritable changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the underlying DNA sequence, often caused by chemical modifications to DNA or histones.
Example: "Epigenetics research has shown that environmental factors such as diet and stress can alter gene expression across generations."
Collocations: epigenetic modification | epigenetic inheritance | epigenetic change

**karyotype** *(noun)* — /KAIR-ee-oh-typ/ (KAIR stressed)
Definition: a visual representation of the full set of chromosomes in a cell, arranged by size and shape to reveal any abnormalities.
Example: "A karyotype analysis can identify conditions such as Down syndrome, which is characterised by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21."
Collocations: karyotype analysis | human karyotype | chromosomal karyotype

**haploid** *(adjective)* — /HAP-loyd/ (HAP stressed)
Definition: describing a cell or organism that contains a single complete set of chromosomes, as found in gametes.
Example: "Haploid gametes produced by meiosis fuse during fertilisation to restore the diploid chromosome number of the offspring."
Collocations: haploid cell | haploid organism | haploid number

**diploid** *(adjective)* — /DIP-loyd/ (DIP stressed)
Definition: describing a cell or organism that contains two complete sets of chromosomes, one inherited from each parent.
Example: "Most body cells in humans are diploid, containing 46 chromosomes organised as 23 homologous pairs."
Collocations: diploid cell | diploid organism | diploid number

**crossing over** *(noun phrase)* — /KROS-ing OH-vur/ (KROS stressed)
Definition: the exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during meiosis, producing new combinations of alleles.
Example: "Crossing over during prophase I of meiosis increases genetic diversity by shuffling alleles between maternal and paternal chromosomes."
Collocations: crossing over in meiosis | crossing over frequency | crossing over and recombination

**locus** *(noun)* — /LOH-kus/ (LOH stressed)
Definition: the specific, fixed position on a chromosome where a particular gene or genetic marker is located.
Example: "Researchers identified the locus of the gene responsible for Huntington's disease on chromosome 4 using linkage analysis."
Collocations: gene locus | chromosomal locus | locus mapping

**heterozygous** *(adjective)* — /het-ur-oh-ZY-gus/ (ZY stressed)
Definition: having two different alleles for a particular gene, one inherited from each parent.
Example: "A heterozygous individual carrying one dominant and one recessive allele for a trait will display the dominant phenotype."
Collocations: heterozygous genotype | heterozygous carrier | heterozygous individual

**homozygous** *(adjective)* — /hoh-moh-ZY-gus/ (ZY stressed)
Definition: having two identical alleles for a particular gene, either both dominant or both recessive.
Example: "Homozygous recessive individuals express the recessive phenotype because no dominant allele is present to mask it."
Collocations: homozygous dominant | homozygous recessive | homozygous genotype

**codominance** *(noun)* — /koh-DOM-ih-nuns/ (DOM stressed)
Definition: a pattern of inheritance in which both alleles of a gene are fully expressed in the phenotype of a heterozygous individual.
Example: "The AB blood type in humans demonstrates codominance because both the A and B antigens are expressed on red blood cell surfaces."
Collocations: codominant allele | codominance pattern | codominance example

**locus** — see entry 061 above.

## Section 4 — Molecular Biology (076–100)

**molecular biology** *(noun)* — /muh-LEK-yuh-lur by-OL-uh-jee/ (LEK and OL stressed)
Definition: the branch of biology that studies the molecular mechanisms underlying biological processes, particularly the structure and function of DNA, RNA, and proteins.
Example: "Molecular biology techniques such as PCR have transformed medical diagnostics by enabling rapid detection of pathogenic DNA."
Collocations: molecular biology technique | molecular biology research | central dogma of molecular biology

**transcription** *(noun)* — /tran-SKRIP-shun/ (SKRIP stressed)
Definition: the first stage of gene expression in which the sequence of a DNA gene is copied into a complementary strand of messenger RNA.
Example: "During transcription, the enzyme RNA polymerase moves along the DNA template strand, assembling a complementary mRNA molecule."
Collocations: gene transcription | transcription factor | transcription rate

**translation** *(noun)* — /trans-LAY-shun/ (LAY stressed)
Definition: the process by which ribosomes decode the sequence of codons in messenger RNA to assemble a corresponding chain of amino acids.
Example: "Translation occurs at the ribosome, where each codon in the mRNA is matched to a specific amino acid delivered by transfer RNA."
Collocations: protein translation | translation initiation | translation rate

**codon** *(noun)* — /KOH-don/ (KOH stressed)
Definition: a sequence of three consecutive nucleotides in mRNA that specifies a particular amino acid or signals the start or stop of translation.
Example: "The start codon AUG signals the ribosome to begin translation, always encoding the amino acid methionine at the protein's N-terminus."
Collocations: start codon | stop codon | codon sequence

**amino acid** *(noun)* — /uh-MEE-noh AS-id/ (MEE and AS stressed)
Definition: one of twenty organic molecules that serve as the building blocks of proteins, each distinguished by a unique side chain.
Example: "The sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain determines how the protein folds and ultimately what biological function it performs."
Collocations: essential amino acid | amino acid sequence | amino acid chain

**polypeptide** *(noun)* — /pol-ee-PEP-tyd/ (PEP stressed)
Definition: a chain of amino acids linked by peptide bonds, which may fold to form a functional protein.
Example: "A polypeptide chain of several hundred amino acids folds into a three-dimensional conformation that determines its enzymatic activity."
Collocations: polypeptide chain | polypeptide synthesis | polypeptide folding

**protein synthesis** *(noun)* — /PROH-teen SIN-thuh-sis/ (PROH and SIN stressed)
Definition: the two-stage process of transcription and translation by which the information encoded in a gene is used to produce a specific protein.
Example: "Antibiotics such as streptomycin inhibit bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 30S subunit of the ribosome."
Collocations: protein synthesis rate | protein synthesis inhibition | ribosomal protein synthesis

**peptide bond** *(noun)* — /PEP-tyd bond/ (PEP stressed)
Definition: a covalent chemical bond formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another, linking amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
Example: "The formation of a peptide bond releases a water molecule in a condensation reaction, which is the reverse of hydrolysis."
Collocations: peptide bond formation | peptide bond cleavage | peptide bond hydrolysis

**denaturation** *(noun)* — /dee-nay-chuh-RAY-shun/ (RAY stressed)
Definition: the irreversible disruption of a protein's three-dimensional structure caused by heat, pH changes, or other environmental factors, leading to loss of function.
Example: "Cooking an egg denatures the albumin proteins, converting the transparent liquid into a firm white solid."
Collocations: protein denaturation | denaturation temperature | heat denaturation

**active site** *(noun)* — /AK-tiv syt/ (AK stressed)
Definition: the specific region of an enzyme where the substrate binds and the catalytic reaction takes place.
Example: "Competitive inhibitors block enzyme activity by binding to the active site in a way that prevents the substrate from attaching."
Collocations: active site binding | enzyme active site | active site specificity

**cofactor** *(noun)* — /KOH-fak-tur/ (KOH stressed)
Definition: a non-protein chemical compound or metal ion required by an enzyme for its catalytic activity.
Example: "Iron acts as a cofactor for haemoglobin, enabling the protein to bind and transport oxygen through the bloodstream."
Collocations: enzyme cofactor | inorganic cofactor | cofactor binding

**coenzyme** *(noun)* — /koh-EN-zym/ (EN stressed)
Definition: an organic molecule, often a vitamin derivative, that works alongside an enzyme to facilitate a biochemical reaction.
Example: "NAD is a coenzyme that carries electrons between reactions in cellular respiration, transferring energy to the electron transport chain."
Collocations: coenzyme function | coenzyme NAD | coenzyme activity

**electron transport chain** *(noun)* — /ih-LEK-tron TRANS-port chayn/ (LEK and TRANS stressed)
Definition: a series of protein complexes in the inner mitochondrial membrane that transfer electrons and use the released energy to synthesise ATP.
Example: "The electron transport chain is the most productive stage of cellular respiration, generating approximately 32–34 molecules of ATP per glucose molecule."
Collocations: electron transport chain reaction | mitochondrial electron transport | electron transport inhibitor

**Krebs cycle** *(noun)* — /krebz SY-kul/ (krebz stressed)
Definition: a series of chemical reactions in the mitochondrial matrix that oxidises acetyl-CoA to release carbon dioxide and generate electron carriers for the electron transport chain.
Example: "The Krebs cycle, also known as the citric acid cycle, produces NADH and FADH₂ that donate electrons to the electron transport chain."
Collocations: Krebs cycle reaction | Krebs cycle substrate | citric acid cycle

**oxidative phosphorylation** *(noun)* — /OK-sih-day-tiv fos-for-ih-LAY-shun/ (OK and LAY stressed)
Definition: the process by which ATP is synthesised using energy derived from the electron transport chain and the proton gradient across the inner mitochondrial membrane.
Example: "Oxidative phosphorylation is inhibited by cyanide, which blocks the final step of the electron transport chain and prevents ATP synthesis."
Collocations: oxidative phosphorylation pathway | ATP from oxidative phosphorylation | mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation

**signal transduction** *(noun)* — /SIG-nul trans-DUK-shun/ (SIG and DUK stressed)
Definition: the process by which a cell converts an extracellular signal, such as a hormone binding to a receptor, into an intracellular response.
Example: "Signal transduction cascades amplify hormonal messages, so a small quantity of adrenaline can trigger a rapid, large-scale metabolic response."
Collocations: signal transduction pathway | cell signal transduction | signal transduction cascade

**cell signalling** *(noun)* — /sel SIG-nul-ing/ (SIG stressed)
Definition: the communication mechanisms by which cells detect and respond to changes in their environment or receive messages from other cells.
Example: "Disruptions in cell signalling pathways are a common feature of cancer, causing cells to proliferate uncontrollably."
Collocations: cell signalling pathway | cell signalling molecule | intracellular signalling

**genomics** *(noun)* — /juh-NOH-miks/ (NOH stressed)
Definition: the branch of molecular biology concerned with the structure, function, evolution, and mapping of genomes.
Example: "Comparative genomics has revealed that humans share approximately 98% of their DNA sequence with chimpanzees."
Collocations: comparative genomics | genomics research | genomic data

**proteomics** *(noun)* — /proh-tee-OH-miks/ (OH stressed)
Definition: the large-scale study of the full complement of proteins expressed by a cell, tissue, or organism at a given time.
Example: "Proteomics research has identified disease-specific protein biomarkers that may enable earlier diagnosis of conditions such as Alzheimer's disease."
Collocations: proteomics analysis | proteomics data | clinical proteomics

**bioinformatics** *(noun)* — /by-oh-in-for-MAT-iks/ (MAT stressed)
Definition: the application of computational tools, statistics, and databases to analyse and interpret biological data, especially large-scale sequence information.
Example: "Bioinformatics software can align thousands of DNA sequences simultaneously, identifying conserved regions that may encode important functional elements."
Collocations: bioinformatics tool | bioinformatics analysis | computational bioinformatics

## Section 5 — Enzymes & Metabolism (101–125)

**enzyme** *(noun)* — /EN-zym/ (EN stressed)
Definition: a biological catalyst, almost always a protein, that accelerates the rate of a specific chemical reaction without being consumed in the process.
Example: "Digestive enzymes such as amylase and lipase break down food molecules into smaller units that can be absorbed across the intestinal wall."
Collocations: enzyme activity | enzyme substrate | enzyme inhibition

**catalyst** *(noun)* — /KAT-uh-list/ (KAT stressed)
Definition: a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction by lowering the activation energy required, without being permanently altered.
Example: "Platinum serves as an inorganic catalyst in catalytic converters, facilitating the conversion of harmful exhaust gases into less toxic compounds."
Collocations: biological catalyst | catalyst reaction | catalytic activity

**substrate** *(noun)* — /SUB-strayt/ (SUB stressed)
Definition: the molecule upon which an enzyme acts, binding to the active site to undergo a chemical transformation.
Example: "The enzyme lactase acts on the substrate lactose, breaking it into glucose and galactose in the small intestine."
Collocations: enzyme substrate | substrate binding | substrate concentration

**inhibitor** *(noun)* — /in-HIB-ih-tur/ (HIB stressed)
Definition: a molecule that reduces or prevents the activity of an enzyme by binding to it, either at the active site or at another regulatory site.
Example: "Many pharmaceutical drugs function as enzyme inhibitors, blocking the activity of proteins involved in disease pathways."
Collocations: enzyme inhibitor | competitive inhibitor | inhibitor concentration

**activation energy** *(noun)* — /ak-tih-VAY-shun EN-ur-jee/ (VAY stressed)
Definition: the minimum amount of energy required to initiate a chemical reaction, which enzymes reduce to allow reactions to proceed more easily.
Example: "By reducing activation energy, enzymes allow metabolic reactions to occur rapidly at body temperature without the need for extreme heat."
Collocations: activation energy barrier | lower activation energy | activation energy diagram

**anabolism** *(noun)* — /uh-NAB-uh-liz-um/ (NAB stressed)
Definition: the set of metabolic reactions that construct larger molecules from smaller ones, requiring an input of energy.
Example: "Protein synthesis is an anabolic process, assembling amino acids into complex polypeptides using the energy stored in ATP molecules."
