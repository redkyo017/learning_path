# Vocabulary: Health & Medicine

**Word count:** 324
**Study method:** Read 20 words per session. For each word: read the definition, cover it, say the definition from memory, then say the example sentence aloud using British RP.

## Section 1: Core Medical Concepts

**health** *(noun)* — /HELTH/
Definition: The overall state of a person's physical, mental, and social well-being, not merely the absence of disease.
Example: "The World Health Organization defines health as a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being."
Collocations: good health | public health | health outcomes

**healthcare** *(noun)* — /HELTH-kair/
Definition: The organised provision of medical services, including prevention, diagnosis, treatment, and recovery, to a population.
Example: "Access to affordable healthcare remains one of the most pressing challenges facing developing nations."
Collocations: healthcare system | healthcare provider | universal healthcare

**medicine** *(noun)* — /MED-ih-sin/
Definition: The science and practice of diagnosing, treating, and preventing illness; also refers to a drug used to treat disease.
Example: "Evidence-based medicine requires that clinical decisions be grounded in the best available research."
Collocations: practice medicine | complementary medicine | preventive medicine

**medical** *(adjective)* — /MED-ih-kul/
Definition: Relating to the science or practice of medicine or the treatment of disease.
Example: "The patient was admitted for a medical assessment after presenting with severe chest pain."
Collocations: medical history | medical record | medical intervention

**clinical** *(adjective)* — /KLIN-ih-kul/
Definition: Relating to the direct examination and treatment of patients, or to a hospital or clinic setting.
Example: "The drug showed promising results in laboratory studies, but clinical trials are needed to confirm safety."
Collocations: clinical trial | clinical setting | clinical evidence

**diagnosis** *(noun)* — /dy-ug-NOH-sis/
Definition: The identification of the nature of an illness or other problem by examining the signs and symptoms.
Example: "An early diagnosis of type 2 diabetes dramatically improves the patient's long-term prognosis."
Collocations: early diagnosis | differential diagnosis | accurate diagnosis

**diagnose** *(verb)* — /DY-ug-nohz/
Definition: To identify the nature of a disease or problem in a patient by examining their symptoms and test results.
Example: "Physicians may struggle to diagnose rare conditions if they have not encountered them before."
Collocations: diagnose a condition | newly diagnosed | misdiagnose

**symptom** *(noun)* — /SIMP-tum/
Definition: A physical or mental feature that a patient notices and reports, which may indicate a disease or health problem.
Example: "Fatigue and unexplained weight loss are symptoms that warrant immediate medical investigation."
Collocations: present symptoms | symptom management | cardinal symptom

**sign** *(noun, medical)* — /SYN/
Definition: An objective, measurable indicator of disease that can be observed or measured by a clinician, as distinct from a subjective symptom.
Example: "A raised temperature is a classic sign of infection that a nurse can measure objectively."
Collocations: vital sign | clinical sign | sign of disease

**syndrome** *(noun)* — /SIN-drohm/
Definition: A group of symptoms and signs that consistently occur together and characterise a particular abnormal condition.
Example: "Metabolic syndrome is a cluster of conditions including high blood pressure, high blood sugar, and excess abdominal fat."
Collocations: define a syndrome | recognise a syndrome | rare syndrome

**disease** *(noun)* — /dih-ZEEZ/
Definition: A disorder of structure or function in a human, animal, or plant that produces specific signs and symptoms.
Example: "Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death globally, accounting for nearly one-third of all mortality."
Collocations: infectious disease | chronic disease | disease burden

**disorder** *(noun)* — /dis-OR-dur/
Definition: A disturbance in physical or mental health or in bodily functions that is not simply a normal response to a situation.
Example: "Bipolar disorder is characterised by extreme mood episodes that alternate between mania and depression."
Collocations: mental disorder | genetic disorder | anxiety disorder

**condition** *(noun, medical)* — /kun-DISH-un/
Definition: A state of health, especially an illness or other medical problem that requires ongoing management.
Example: "Asthma is a long-term condition that can be effectively controlled with the correct inhaled medication."
Collocations: underlying condition | pre-existing condition | medical condition

**chronic** *(adjective)* — /KRON-ik/
Definition: Describing a disease or condition that persists for a long time or recurs frequently, as opposed to being acute.
Example: "Chronic back pain affects millions of workers and is a leading cause of occupational disability worldwide."
Collocations: chronic illness | chronic pain | chronic condition

**acute** *(adjective)* — /uh-KYOOT/
Definition: Describing a disease or symptom that has a sudden onset, is severe, and is of short duration.
Example: "The patient was rushed to the emergency department with acute appendicitis requiring immediate surgery."
Collocations: acute care | acute episode | acute phase

**terminal** *(adjective)* — /TUR-mih-nul/
Definition: Describing a disease or condition that cannot be cured and is expected to lead to death within a limited period.
Example: "Patients with terminal cancer often prioritise quality of life over aggressive curative treatment."
Collocations: terminal illness | terminal diagnosis | terminal stage

**benign** *(adjective)* — /bih-NYN/
Definition: Describing a tumour or growth that is not cancerous and does not spread to other tissues.
Example: "Further imaging confirmed the growth was benign, eliminating the need for chemotherapy."
Collocations: benign tumour | benign condition | benign growth

**malignant** *(adjective)* — /muh-LIG-nunt/
Definition: Describing a tumour or disease that tends to invade and destroy surrounding tissue and spread to other parts of the body.
Example: "The biopsy revealed malignant cells, confirming the diagnosis of breast cancer."
Collocations: malignant tumour | malignant melanoma | malignant cells

**pathology** *(noun)* — /puh-THOL-uh-jee/
Definition: The branch of medicine concerned with the causes, nature, and effects of diseases, especially as revealed through laboratory examination.
Example: "Pathology reports from the tissue sample revealed the presence of abnormal cellular activity."
Collocations: clinical pathology | pathology report | speech pathology

**pathogen** *(noun)* — /PATH-uh-jen/
Definition: A bacterium, virus, or other microorganism that can cause disease in its host.
Example: "Understanding how a pathogen enters and replicates within the body is fundamental to developing targeted therapies."
Collocations: pathogen transmission | infectious pathogen | emerging pathogen

**prognosis** *(noun)* — /prog-NOH-sis/
Definition: The likely course and outcome of a disease, based on general medical knowledge combined with the patient's specific situation.
Example: "The patient's prognosis improved significantly following early surgical intervention."
Collocations: poor prognosis | favourable prognosis | long-term prognosis

**aetiology** *(noun)* — /ee-tee-OL-uh-jee/
Definition: The cause or set of causes of a disease or condition, as studied in medical research.
Example: "The aetiology of type 1 diabetes involves both genetic predisposition and environmental triggers."
Collocations: unknown aetiology | multifactorial aetiology | investigate the aetiology

**morbidity** *(noun)* — /mor-BID-ih-tee/
Definition: The state of being diseased or unhealthy within a population; also used to describe the rate of disease in a population.
Example: "Reducing morbidity from preventable diseases is a core objective of national public health strategies."
Collocations: morbidity rate | reduce morbidity | morbidity and mortality

**mortality** *(noun, medical)* — /mor-TAL-ih-tee/
Definition: The rate at which people die from a particular disease or condition within a given period or population.
Example: "Improved surgical techniques have dramatically reduced post-operative mortality in cardiac patients."
Collocations: mortality rate | infant mortality | excess mortality

**prevalence** *(noun)* — /PREV-uh-luns/
Definition: The proportion of a population found to have a particular condition at a specific point in time.
Example: "The prevalence of obesity has tripled in many countries since the 1980s."
Collocations: high prevalence | disease prevalence | lifetime prevalence

**incidence** *(noun)* — /IN-sih-duns/
Definition: The frequency with which a disease or condition appears in a population over a specified period.
Example: "The incidence of lung cancer declined sharply following decades of anti-smoking public health campaigns."
Collocations: incidence rate | cumulative incidence | rising incidence

**risk factor** *(noun)* — /RISK FAK-tur/
Definition: A characteristic or behaviour that increases an individual's probability of developing a particular disease.
Example: "Smoking, high cholesterol, and physical inactivity are all well-established risk factors for cardiovascular disease."
Collocations: modifiable risk factor | risk factor reduction | identify risk factors

**comorbidity** *(noun)* — /koh-mor-BID-ih-tee/
Definition: The simultaneous presence of two or more chronic diseases or conditions in the same patient.
Example: "Patients with diabetes frequently experience comorbidities such as hypertension and kidney disease."
Collocations: manage comorbidity | multiple comorbidities | comorbid condition

**complication** *(noun, medical)* — /kom-plih-KAY-shun/
Definition: A secondary disease or condition that develops during the course of or as a consequence of a primary disease or treatment.
Example: "Deep vein thrombosis is a serious complication that can arise following prolonged immobility after surgery."
Collocations: medical complication | post-operative complication | develop complications

**relapse** *(noun/verb)* — /REE-laps/
Definition: The return of disease signs and symptoms after a period of improvement or apparent recovery.
Example: "Patients who stop taking their antidepressants prematurely are at greater risk of relapse within six months."
Collocations: disease relapse | prevent relapse | risk of relapse

## Section 2: Infectious Disease

**virus** *(noun)* — /VY-rus/
Definition: A microscopic infectious agent that replicates only inside the living cells of a host organism.
Example: "The influenza virus mutates rapidly, which is why annual vaccination updates are necessary."
Collocations: viral infection | virus replication | respiratory virus

**bacteria** *(noun, plural)* — /bak-TEER-ee-uh/
Definition: Single-celled microorganisms, some of which can cause infectious diseases in humans and animals.
Example: "Unlike viruses, bacterial infections can typically be treated with a targeted course of antibiotics."
Collocations: bacterial infection | harmful bacteria | gut bacteria

**fungal** *(adjective)* — /FUNG-gul/
Definition: Relating to or caused by a fungus; used to describe infections caused by fungal organisms.
Example: "Immunocompromised patients are particularly susceptible to fungal infections such as candidiasis."
Collocations: fungal infection | fungal pathogen | antifungal treatment

**parasite** *(noun)* — /PAIR-uh-syt/
Definition: An organism that lives on or in another organism and benefits at the host's expense, often causing disease.
Example: "Malaria is caused by a Plasmodium parasite that is transmitted through the bite of infected mosquitoes."
Collocations: parasitic infection | intestinal parasite | parasite lifecycle

**infection** *(noun)* — /in-FEK-shun/
Definition: The invasion and multiplication of pathogenic microorganisms in body tissues, producing a response in the host.
Example: "Surgical site infections are a preventable cause of increased hospital stays and healthcare costs."
Collocations: bacterial infection | treat an infection | opportunistic infection

**contagion** *(noun)* — /kun-TAY-jun/
Definition: The communication of disease from one person to another through direct or indirect contact.
Example: "Fear of contagion led authorities to implement strict border controls during the early days of the outbreak."
Collocations: risk of contagion | spread contagion | contagion control

**contagious** *(adjective)* — /kun-TAY-jus/
Definition: Capable of being transmitted from one person to another through direct or indirect contact.
Example: "Measles is highly contagious and can spread through the air even before symptoms appear in the infected individual."
Collocations: highly contagious | contagious disease | contagious period

**epidemic** *(noun)* — /ep-ih-DEM-ik/
Definition: A widespread occurrence of an infectious disease in a community at a particular time, above the expected level.
Example: "The cholera epidemic swept through the coastal region after flooding contaminated local water supplies."
Collocations: epidemic outbreak | control an epidemic | epidemic curve

**pandemic** *(noun)* — /pan-DEM-ik/
Definition: An epidemic of infectious disease that has spread across a large region, multiple continents, or worldwide.
Example: "The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated the critical importance of international cooperation in public health preparedness."
Collocations: global pandemic | pandemic response | pandemic preparedness

**endemic** *(adjective, medical)* — /en-DEM-ik/
Definition: Describing a disease regularly found among particular people or in a certain area, at a consistently high level.
Example: "Malaria is endemic in sub-Saharan Africa, where it remains a leading cause of death in children under five."
Collocations: endemic disease | endemic region | endemic infection

**outbreak** *(noun)* — /OWT-brayk/
Definition: A sudden occurrence of a disease in a particular time and place, affecting a number of people simultaneously.
Example: "Health authorities investigated the foodborne outbreak that affected over three hundred restaurant patrons."
Collocations: disease outbreak | investigate an outbreak | contain an outbreak

**transmission** *(noun)* — /trans-MISH-un/
Definition: The passing of a disease from an infected individual or group to another, directly or indirectly.
Example: "Transmission of the virus was effectively reduced through consistent mask-wearing and improved ventilation."
Collocations: disease transmission | airborne transmission | mode of transmission

**vector** *(noun)* — /VEK-tur/
Definition: An organism, typically an insect, that transmits a pathogen from one host to another without itself being affected.
Example: "The Aedes aegypti mosquito serves as the primary vector for dengue fever, Zika, and yellow fever."
Collocations: disease vector | vector control | vector-borne disease

**host** *(noun, biology)* — /HOHST/
Definition: An organism in which a parasite or pathogen lives and from which it obtains nourishment.
Example: "The virus cannot survive outside its host for more than a few hours under normal environmental conditions."
Collocations: host organism | definitive host | host immune response

**incubation** *(noun)* — /in-kyoo-BAY-shun/
Definition: The period between exposure to an infection and the first appearance of signs and symptoms.
Example: "The incubation period for COVID-19 is typically two to fourteen days, complicating early containment efforts."
Collocations: incubation period | short incubation | extend the incubation period

**latency** *(noun)* — /LAY-ten-see/
Definition: The period during which an infection is present in the body but produces no noticeable symptoms.
Example: "HIV may remain in a state of latency for years before progressing to AIDS without antiretroviral treatment."
Collocations: latency period | viral latency | emerge from latency

**sepsis** *(noun)* — /SEP-sis/
Definition: A life-threatening condition caused by the body's overwhelming and dysregulated response to an infection.
Example: "Unrecognised sepsis can progress rapidly to organ failure, making early detection critical for patient survival."
Collocations: septic shock | develop sepsis | early sepsis

**inflammation** *(noun)* — /in-fluh-MAY-shun/
Definition: A biological response of body tissues to harmful stimuli such as infection or injury, typically causing redness, heat, and pain.
Example: "Chronic low-grade inflammation is now understood to play a central role in the development of atherosclerosis."
Collocations: systemic inflammation | reduce inflammation | inflammation marker

**fever** *(noun)* — /FEE-vur/
Definition: An abnormally high body temperature, usually above 38°C, typically caused by infection or illness.
Example: "A persistent fever lasting more than three days in a young child should prompt an urgent medical evaluation."
Collocations: high fever | develop a fever | fever management

**allergy** *(noun)* — /AL-ur-jee/
Definition: An abnormal immune response to a normally harmless substance such as pollen, food, or medication.
Example: "The prevalence of food allergies in children has risen considerably over the past three decades."
Collocations: food allergy | allergic reaction | allergy testing

**anaphylaxis** *(noun)* — /an-uh-fih-LAK-sis/
Definition: A severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that requires immediate treatment with adrenaline.
Example: "The patient experienced anaphylaxis after eating peanuts and required emergency adrenaline administration."
Collocations: anaphylactic shock | risk of anaphylaxis | treat anaphylaxis

**autoimmune** *(adjective)* — /aw-toh-ih-MYOON/
Definition: Relating to a condition in which the body's immune system mistakenly attacks its own healthy cells and tissues.
Example: "Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system destroys insulin-producing cells."
Collocations: autoimmune disease | autoimmune response | autoimmune condition

**antibody** *(noun, medicine)* — /AN-tih-bod-ee/
Definition: A protein produced by the immune system that recognises and neutralises a specific antigen such as a bacterium or virus.
Example: "The presence of antibodies in the blood indicates that the individual has been exposed to or vaccinated against the pathogen."
Collocations: produce antibodies | antibody test | neutralising antibody

**immune response** *(noun phrase)* — /ih-MYOON rih-SPONS/
Definition: The activity of the immune system in detecting and destroying foreign substances, including pathogens and abnormal cells.
Example: "Vaccines work by triggering an immune response that prepares the body to fight future encounters with the real pathogen."
Collocations: mount an immune response | adaptive immune response | immune response activation

**vaccine** *(noun)* — /vak-SEEN/
Definition: A biological preparation that provides immunity to a specific disease by stimulating the immune system.
Example: "The rapid development of mRNA vaccines during the COVID-19 pandemic marked a breakthrough in vaccinology."
Collocations: receive a vaccine | vaccine efficacy | live attenuated vaccine

**immunisation** *(noun)* — /im-yoo-nih-ZAY-shun/
Definition: The process by which a person is made immune or resistant to an infectious disease, typically through vaccination.
Example: "Childhood immunisation programmes have nearly eradicated diseases such as polio in many parts of the world."
Collocations: immunisation schedule | routine immunisation | immunisation coverage

**herd immunity** *(noun phrase)* — /HURD ih-MYOO-nih-tee/
Definition: The indirect protection from an infectious disease that occurs when a sufficiently large proportion of a population has become immune.
Example: "Achieving herd immunity through vaccination requires a coverage threshold that varies by disease transmissibility."
Collocations: herd immunity threshold | achieve herd immunity | natural herd immunity

**antibiotic** *(noun)* — /an-tih-by-OT-ik/
Definition: A medicine that inhibits the growth of or destroys bacteria, used to treat bacterial infections.
Example: "The inappropriate use of antibiotics in agriculture has accelerated the emergence of drug-resistant bacteria."
Collocations: prescribe antibiotics | antibiotic course | broad-spectrum antibiotic

**antiviral** *(noun/adjective)* — /an-tih-VY-rul/
Definition: A medication that inhibits the development or replication of viruses, used to treat viral infections.
Example: "Early administration of antiviral medication can significantly reduce the severity and duration of influenza."
Collocations: antiviral therapy | antiviral drug | antiviral resistance

**antibiotic resistance** *(noun phrase)* — /an-tih-by-OT-ik rih-ZIS-tuns/
Definition: The ability of bacteria to survive and multiply despite the presence of antibiotics designed to kill them.
Example: "Antibiotic resistance poses one of the greatest threats to global health, making previously treatable infections potentially fatal."
Collocations: rise of antibiotic resistance | combat antibiotic resistance | antibiotic resistance mechanism

**superbugs** *(noun, plural)* — /SOO-pur-bugz/
Definition: Informal term for bacteria that have developed resistance to multiple antibiotics, making them extremely difficult to treat.
Example: "Superbugs such as carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae are spreading through healthcare facilities worldwide."
Collocations: drug-resistant superbugs | fight superbugs | spread of superbugs

**MRSA** *(noun, abbreviation)* — /em-ar-es-AY/ or /MUR-suh/
Definition: Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus; a bacterium resistant to many common antibiotics and associated with serious hospital-acquired infections.
Example: "Strict hand hygiene protocols in hospitals are among the most effective measures for preventing MRSA transmission."
Collocations: MRSA infection | MRSA outbreak | screen for MRSA

**zoonotic disease** *(noun phrase)* — /zoh-uh-NOT-ik dih-ZEEZ/
Definition: An infectious disease that has jumped from a non-human animal to humans, either directly or through an intermediary host.
Example: "Ebola, SARS, and rabies are all examples of zoonotic diseases that originate in animal reservoirs."
Collocations: zoonotic transmission | emerging zoonotic disease | prevent zoonotic spillover

**tuberculosis** *(noun)* — /tyoo-bur-kyoo-LOH-sis/
Definition: A serious infectious bacterial disease, primarily of the lungs, caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis and spread through the air.
Example: "Despite being preventable and curable, tuberculosis remains the world's leading cause of death from a single infectious agent."
Collocations: latent tuberculosis | active tuberculosis | tuberculosis treatment

**malaria** *(noun)* — /muh-LAIR-ee-uh/
Definition: A life-threatening disease caused by Plasmodium parasites, transmitted to humans through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes.
Example: "Insecticide-treated bed nets are a cost-effective intervention for reducing malaria transmission in endemic regions."
Collocations: malaria prevention | malaria endemic | malaria parasite

**influenza** *(noun)* — /in-floo-EN-zuh/
Definition: A highly contagious viral infection of the respiratory tract, commonly known as flu, causing fever, muscle aches, and fatigue.
Example: "Annual influenza vaccination is especially recommended for the elderly, young children, and pregnant women."
Collocations: influenza virus | influenza vaccine | influenza season

**HIV/AIDS** *(noun phrase)* — /aych-eye-VEE / AYDZ/
Definition: Human Immunodeficiency Virus causes AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome), a condition that severely weakens the immune system.
Example: "Antiretroviral therapy has transformed HIV/AIDS from a fatal diagnosis into a manageable chronic condition."
Collocations: HIV transmission | HIV status | AIDS-defining illness

**hepatitis** *(noun)* — /hep-uh-TY-tis/
Definition: Inflammation of the liver, typically caused by a viral infection (hepatitis A, B, or C) or by toxins such as alcohol.
Example: "Hepatitis B vaccination is recommended in infancy to prevent chronic liver disease and hepatocellular carcinoma."
Collocations: hepatitis B | chronic hepatitis | hepatitis transmission

**pneumonia** *(noun)* — /nyoo-MOH-nyuh/
Definition: A lung infection that causes inflammation of the air sacs, which may fill with fluid or pus, causing cough, fever, and difficulty breathing.
Example: "Community-acquired pneumonia is a leading cause of hospital admission in both the young and elderly populations."
Collocations: bacterial pneumonia | treat pneumonia | pneumonia vaccination

## Section 3: Cardiovascular & Metabolic Disease

**cardiovascular** *(adjective)* — /kar-dee-oh-VAS-kyoo-lur/
Definition: Relating to the heart and blood vessels, and the diseases and conditions that affect them.
Example: "Regular aerobic exercise significantly reduces cardiovascular risk by lowering blood pressure and improving lipid profiles."
Collocations: cardiovascular disease | cardiovascular risk | cardiovascular health

**hypertension** *(noun)* — /hy-pur-TEN-shun/
Definition: A condition in which blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.
Example: "Hypertension is often called the silent killer because it typically presents without noticeable symptoms."
Collocations: essential hypertension | manage hypertension | hypertension treatment

**atherosclerosis** *(noun)* — /ath-ur-oh-skluh-ROH-sis/
Definition: A disease in which fatty plaques accumulate inside arterial walls, narrowing and hardening the arteries over time.
Example: "Atherosclerosis develops silently over decades and is the underlying cause of most heart attacks and strokes."
Collocations: atherosclerotic plaque | atherosclerosis progression | coronary atherosclerosis

**stroke** *(noun)* — /STROHK/
Definition: A medical emergency in which blood supply to part of the brain is suddenly interrupted, causing rapid onset of neurological deficits.
Example: "Recognising the early signs of stroke using the FAST acronym can significantly reduce permanent brain damage."
Collocations: ischaemic stroke | stroke rehabilitation | stroke prevention

**heart attack** *(noun)* — /HART AT-tak/
Definition: A sudden blockage of blood flow to the heart muscle, causing damage or death to part of the myocardium.
Example: "Aspirin given immediately during a suspected heart attack can reduce the extent of myocardial damage."
Collocations: heart attack risk | survive a heart attack | heart attack symptoms

**myocardial infarction** *(noun phrase)* — /my-oh-KAR-dee-ul in-FARK-shun/
Definition: The medical term for a heart attack; death of myocardial tissue caused by ischaemia due to a blocked coronary artery.
Example: "Troponin levels in the blood are the primary biomarker used to confirm or exclude myocardial infarction."
Collocations: acute myocardial infarction | myocardial infarction diagnosis | non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction

**arrhythmia** *(noun)* — /uh-RITH-mee-uh/
Definition: An abnormality in the rate or rhythm of the heartbeat, which may be harmless or potentially life-threatening.
Example: "Atrial fibrillation is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia and a major risk factor for stroke."
Collocations: cardiac arrhythmia | treat arrhythmia | arrhythmia management

**cholesterol** *(noun)* — /kuh-LES-tur-ol/
Definition: A fatty substance found in the blood; elevated levels of low-density lipoprotein cholesterol increase cardiovascular disease risk.
Example: "A diet low in saturated fat is among the lifestyle modifications recommended to reduce cholesterol levels."
Collocations: high cholesterol | LDL cholesterol | lower cholesterol

**diabetes** *(noun)* — /dy-uh-BEE-teez/
Definition: A chronic metabolic disease characterised by elevated blood glucose levels due to insufficient insulin production or action.
Example: "Type 2 diabetes can often be prevented or delayed through dietary changes, physical activity, and weight management."
Collocations: type 2 diabetes | diabetes management | diabetes complications

**insulin** *(noun)* — /IN-syoo-lin/
Definition: A hormone produced by the pancreas that allows cells to absorb glucose from the blood and use it for energy.
Example: "Patients with type 1 diabetes require insulin injections because their pancreas produces none of the hormone."
Collocations: insulin resistance | inject insulin | insulin therapy

**glucose** *(noun)* — /GLOO-kohs/
Definition: A simple sugar that is the primary source of energy for the body's cells, regulated by insulin in the bloodstream.
Example: "Fasting plasma glucose above 7.0 mmol/L on two separate occasions confirms the diagnosis of diabetes."
Collocations: blood glucose | glucose level | glucose metabolism

**obesity** *(noun)* — /oh-BEE-sih-tee/
Definition: A medical condition characterised by excessive body fat that presents significant health risks, defined by a BMI over 30.
Example: "Obesity is associated with an increased risk of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and several cancers."
Collocations: childhood obesity | obesity prevention | morbid obesity

**metabolic syndrome** *(noun phrase)* — /met-uh-BOL-ik SIN-drohm/
Definition: A cluster of five interrelated conditions — excess abdominal fat, high blood pressure, high blood sugar, high triglycerides, and low HDL — that together increase disease risk.
Example: "Metabolic syndrome affects roughly one-quarter of the global adult population and significantly raises the risk of heart disease."
Collocations: diagnose metabolic syndrome | metabolic syndrome criteria | manage metabolic syndrome

**chronic disease** *(noun phrase)* — /KRON-ik dih-ZEEZ/
Definition: A long-lasting health condition that can be controlled but not cured, typically requiring ongoing medical management.
Example: "Chronic diseases now account for the majority of global mortality and represent a growing burden on health systems."
Collocations: chronic disease management | chronic disease prevention | burden of chronic disease

**sedentary** *(adjective)* — /SED-un-ter-ee/
Definition: Characterised by much sitting and little physical activity; used to describe a lifestyle that contributes to poor health outcomes.
Example: "A sedentary lifestyle is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, regardless of other health behaviours."
Collocations: sedentary behaviour | sedentary lifestyle | sedentary occupation

**physical activity** *(noun phrase)* — /FIZ-ih-kul ak-TIV-ih-tee/
Definition: Any bodily movement produced by skeletal muscles that requires energy expenditure, ranging from exercise to daily walking.
Example: "The WHO recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity physical activity per week for adults."
Collocations: physical activity guidelines | lack of physical activity | promote physical activity

**diet** *(noun, medical)* — /DY-ut/
Definition: The food and drink regularly consumed by a person, and the nutritional habits considered in relation to their health effects.
Example: "A Mediterranean diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and olive oil is associated with a reduced risk of cardiovascular disease."
Collocations: balanced diet | dietary intervention | healthy diet

**nutrition** *(noun)* — /nyoo-TRISH-un/
Definition: The process of providing or obtaining the food necessary for health and growth; also the scientific study of food and its effects.
Example: "Adequate nutrition in the first 1,000 days of life has lifelong consequences for a child's cognitive development."
Collocations: proper nutrition | nutritional status | clinical nutrition

**caloric** *(adjective)* — /kuh-LOR-ik/
Definition: Relating to heat produced by food as measured in calories; concerning the energy content of food.
Example: "A caloric deficit, created by consuming less energy than the body expends, is the fundamental mechanism behind weight loss."
Collocations: caloric intake | caloric restriction | caloric balance

**microbiome** *(noun)* — /my-kroh-BY-ohm/
Definition: The community of microorganisms — bacteria, fungi, and viruses — that lives in a particular environment, especially the human gut.
Example: "Emerging research suggests that the gut microbiome plays a critical role in regulating immune function and mental health."
Collocations: gut microbiome | microbiome diversity | microbiome research

**gut bacteria** *(noun phrase)* — /GUT bak-TEER-ee-uh/
Definition: The trillions of bacteria that reside in the intestinal tract and play essential roles in digestion, immunity, and metabolism.
Example: "Disruption of gut bacteria through antibiotic use can lead to gastrointestinal problems and increased susceptibility to infection."
Collocations: beneficial gut bacteria | gut bacteria balance | gut bacteria diversity

**probiotic** *(noun)* — /proh-by-OT-ik/
Definition: A live microorganism that, when administered in adequate amounts, confers a health benefit on the host, particularly through gut health.
Example: "Clinical trials have shown that probiotics can reduce the duration of antibiotic-associated diarrhoea."
Collocations: probiotic supplement | probiotic therapy | probiotic strain

**blood pressure** *(noun phrase)* — /BLUD PRESH-ur/
Definition: The pressure exerted by circulating blood against the walls of the arteries, measured in millimetres of mercury.
Example: "Regular monitoring of blood pressure is essential for early detection of hypertension in at-risk populations."
Collocations: high blood pressure | blood pressure reading | blood pressure medication

**BMI** *(noun, abbreviation)* — /bee-em-EYE/
Definition: Body Mass Index; a measure of body fat based on weight and height used to classify underweight, healthy weight, and obesity.
Example: "While BMI is a useful screening tool, it does not distinguish between fat mass and muscle mass in individuals."
Collocations: calculate BMI | BMI category | BMI threshold

**artery** *(noun)* — /AR-tur-ee/
Definition: A blood vessel that carries oxygenated blood away from the heart to the body's tissues.
Example: "Narrowing of the coronary arteries due to plaque build-up is the primary cause of ischaemic heart disease."
Collocations: coronary artery | clogged artery | arterial disease

**hormone** *(noun)* — /HOR-mohn/
Definition: A chemical messenger produced by glands and secreted into the bloodstream to regulate physiological processes in distant organs.
Example: "Thyroid hormones regulate metabolism, heart rate, and body temperature throughout the body."
Collocations: hormone levels | sex hormone | hormone therapy

## Section 4: Cancer & Oncology

**cancer** *(noun)* — /KAN-sur/
Definition: A group of diseases involving uncontrolled cell division and growth that can invade adjacent tissue and spread to distant sites.
Example: "Screening programmes for bowel and breast cancer have contributed to significant reductions in cancer-related mortality."
